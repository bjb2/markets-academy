## A government balance sheet

A government has three main sources of funding:

1. **Taxes** (current claim on real economic activity).
2. **Debt issuance** (claim on future tax revenue, or future debt issuance — refinancing).
3. **Money creation** (the central bank monetizes some of the debt, if the institutional setup allows).

The **deficit** is the gap between spending and tax revenue in a given year. The **debt** is the cumulative stock of unpaid past deficits. Deficits add to debt; surpluses subtract from it.

For the US in 2026, federal debt held by the public is roughly $30T against ~$28T GDP — a debt/GDP ratio around 105–110%. Annual deficit is running ~5–7% of GDP in a non-recession year, which is historically extreme outside wartime.

The conditions for the math to work over decades:

- Nominal GDP growth must exceed the average interest rate on the debt (r < g).
- Primary deficit (deficit ex interest costs) must be manageable.
- Currency must remain credible as a store of value.

When `r > g`, debt/GDP rises mechanically every year you run any primary deficit. This is the structural concern most macro voices raise about the current US setup.

## Why this matters for portfolios

Sovereign debt sustainability shows up in markets through several channels:

- **Term premium**. Long-end Treasury yields embed a "will this government inflate/default/struggle to refinance" premium. Rising debt-to-GDP can push term premia higher even with stable short rates.
- **Currency**. Persistent fiscal stress historically correlates with weaker currency (the dollar is the global reserve, so this works differently for the US than for others — but the same logic applies).
- **Hard assets**. Gold and bitcoin have arguments rooted in fiscal-dominance fears (the "debasement trade" Alden writes about). Whether the arguments are right is contested; that they MATTER for some asset prices is not.
- **Cross-border flows**. Foreign holdings of US Treasuries (~$8T) are politically and financially relevant. If those holders pull back, the marginal buyer changes — and per Layer 0.2, the marginal buyer is everything.
