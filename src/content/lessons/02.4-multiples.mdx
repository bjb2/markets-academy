## The three core multiples

| Multiple | Formula | What it captures | What it ignores |
|---|---|---|---|
| **P/E** | Price / Earnings per share | How many years of earnings to pay back the stock | Capital structure, growth, non-cash charges |
| **EV/EBITDA** | Enterprise value / EBITDA | Operating performance independent of leverage | Capex differences, debt-driven returns |
| **P/B** | Price / Book value per share | Premium to accounting net assets | Intangibles, asset productivity, growth |

Plus a few specialized siblings:
- **P/S** (price-to-sales): for unprofitable companies where E and EBITDA are missing/negative.
- **PEG** (P/E ÷ growth): rough adjustment for growth expectations.
- **EV/EBIT**: like EV/EBITDA but penalizes capex-heavy businesses appropriately (depreciation matters).
- **P/FCF**: price-to-free-cash-flow, closer to economic reality but harder to standardize across companies.

## P/E in detail

P/E = market price / earnings per share. Equivalent: market cap / total net income.

The reciprocal, **earnings yield** = E/P, is the implied return if earnings flow through 1-for-1 to shareholders (which they don't, due to retention vs. distribution choices).

Three flavors:

- **Trailing P/E**: using last 12 months of earnings. Backward-looking; reflects actual results.
- **Forward P/E**: using consensus estimated earnings for next 12 months. Forward-looking; analyst estimates are biased optimistic.
- **Shiller (CAPE) P/E**: using 10-year average inflation-adjusted earnings. Cyclically adjusted; smooths through booms and busts.

Historical S&P 500 P/E has averaged 15-18x over long periods, with notable ranges:
- 5-9x in deeply distressed periods (1933, 1949, 1981, 2008 lows)
- 15-20x in normal periods
- 25-35x in expansionary booms (1999, 2021)
- 40-100x for individual hot stocks (NVDA at various points, Tesla circa 2020)

Headline P/E without context is meaningless. The same 25x can be expensive (mature staples) or cheap (high-growth tech).

## EV/EBITDA in detail

Enterprise Value = market cap + total debt - cash and marketable securities. EV represents what you'd pay to acquire the entire business, including assuming its debt and getting its cash.

EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortization. It strips out:
- Capital structure effects (interest)
- Tax differences (corporate tax rate, jurisdiction)
- Non-cash accounting charges (depreciation, amortization)

The result: EBITDA approximates operating cash flow before capex. It's the most apples-to-apples profitability metric across companies with different capital structures, tax positions, and accounting choices.

EV/EBITDA is the dominant multiple in:
- Private equity acquisition pricing (almost always "Xx EBITDA" deals).
- Cross-border comparisons (tax-neutralized).
- Cross-industry comparisons (capital-structure-neutralized).

The Buffett critique of EBITDA, famously: "EBITDA is what you have left if you take out the parts you don't want to think about." Specifically, depreciation IS a real economic cost (you have to replace assets), so excluding it can mask businesses that look profitable on EBITDA but actually consume cash on capex. The fix: use **EV/EBIT** (which keeps depreciation) for capital-intensive businesses, or directly look at P/FCF.

## P/B in detail

P/B = market cap / book equity. Book equity = total assets - total liabilities, the accounting net worth.

Historically influential because, in the 1930s-1950s, book value approximated liquidation value, providing a "floor" for valuation. Graham's "net-net" strategy (buy stocks trading below 2/3 of liquidation value) explicitly used this anchor.

Today, P/B works for asset-heavy businesses where book value is meaningful (banks, insurance, REITs, basic manufacturing) and is largely useless for asset-light businesses (software, services, brands, IP-heavy firms).

The reason: GAAP doesn't capitalize internally-developed intangibles. Apple's brand, Google's search algorithm, McKinsey's reputation: all immensely valuable, all carry near-zero book value. P/B for these companies is mechanically high regardless of how cheap they really are. The Fama-French value premium (Layer 3.5) is computed on book-to-market and has weakened substantially in recent decades partly because of the rise of intangible-intensive companies.

## When multiples lie

Three common pitfalls:

1. **Single-period earnings are noisy**. A company with $1B normalized earnings might earn $0.2B in a recession year. Trailing P/E spikes from 15x to 75x while the actual business hasn't changed. Use multi-year averages (CAPE) or normalized earnings.

2. **Accounting choices matter**. Two companies with identical economics can report different earnings depending on revenue recognition, inventory method, depreciation schedule. Cross-company comparisons require footnote-level checking, not just headline numbers.

3. **One-time items distort EBITDA more than P/E**. Restructuring charges, gains on asset sales, write-downs all show up in EBITDA. "Adjusted EBITDA" (what management presents) often strips these out, but the strip-out can be aggressive. Cross-check against P/FCF.

A useful practitioner habit: compute SEVERAL multiples for the same company, and if they tell different stories, ask why. The differences are the diagnostic.
