## What factor investing is

Multi-factor investing systematically tilts a portfolio toward documented factor exposures. Two implementations:

### 1. Multi-factor ETFs

A single ETF holding a basket constructed to overweight names with positive scores on multiple factors. Examples:
- VFMF (Vanguard US Multi-Factor): value + momentum + quality
- LRGF (BlackRock US Equity Multi-Factor): similar combination
- QUAL (iShares MSCI USA Quality Factor): single-factor focus on quality
- VLUE, MTUM, USMV, SIZE: single-factor ETFs for value, momentum, low-vol, small-cap

Multi-factor ETFs typically have expense ratios 0.15-0.30%. They mechanically rebalance toward factor exposures quarterly or annually.

### 2. Long-short factor portfolios

Hedge fund and institutional vehicles that go long the top-decile factor names and short the bottom-decile. Generates the "pure factor premium" without market beta. Example: AQR Style Premia Alternative Fund.

Long-short is more expensive (1-1.5% expense ratios; some have 20% performance fees), but provides true factor exposure independent of market direction. Long-only multi-factor ETFs have both factor and market beta; long-short have only factor.

## Empirical evidence (and the compression problem)

Pre-publication (1993-2010): documented factor premia of 4-8% annualized for individual factors (value, size, momentum, quality, low-vol).

Post-publication (2010-2024): compressed to 1-4% annualized for the same factors. The compression mechanism is well-understood:

1. **Academic paper documents premium**.
2. **Practitioners launch products to harvest it** (ETFs, factor funds).
3. **Inflows compress the premium** as the underlying mispricing gets traded out.
4. **Steady state**: smaller premium that survives because someone has to bear the residual risk.

This pattern has been observed for:
- Size premium (worked 1965-1980, mostly disappeared post-publication)
- Value premium (worked through 2007, weakened 2010-2020, partial revival 2021+)
- Quality premium (more stable; high-ROIC has continued to work)
- Low-vol premium (one of the most durable; still working in modern data)
- Momentum premium (working but with notable crashes)

The compression isn't elimination. Most documented factors still deliver positive risk-adjusted returns; just lower than pre-publication backtests suggested.

## How to combine factors

The empirical AMP (2013) result: factors have moderate correlations with each other (typically 0.0-0.4 in long-run data) but are NEGATIVELY correlated in some regimes. A naive equal-weight combination of value + momentum + quality + low-vol historically:

- Reduces standalone volatility by 20-30% vs single-factor.
- Increases risk-adjusted return (Sharpe) by 15-25% vs single-factor.
- Reduces max drawdown materially (single-factor drawdowns of 30-40% reduce to 15-25%).

The lesson from lesson 3.8 generalizes: combining low-correlation factor exposures improves the Sharpe of the portfolio. Smart beta = applied lesson 3.8.

## The factor investing critique

Three honest concerns:

### 1. Publication bias and out-of-sample failure

Many published factors have failed out-of-sample. Harvey, Liu, Zhu (2016) catalog 300+ published factors and estimate that 50-80% are spurious. The robust subset (market, value, momentum, quality, low-vol, size, profitability, investment) is much smaller. Be skeptical of "newly discovered" factors with thin out-of-sample evidence.

### 2. Compression and capacity

The most-traded factor strategies have seen their premia compress. AQR's institutional factor funds (which were star performers in early 2010s) have produced disappointing returns 2018-2023 as the underlying premia compressed. The "smart beta" allocation industry crossed $1.5T by 2024, large enough to compress the premia they harvest.

### 3. Factor exposures are unstable

The empirical loadings on factors change over time. A "value" portfolio in 2024 contains different stocks than a "value" portfolio in 2014; the factor definition itself has evolved (book-to-market alone vs multi-metric value). Investors may not always know what they're buying through a factor ETF; the labels are convenient but the underlying exposures shift.

## How factor investing fits in our framework

For our paper-trading-longcall project, factor investing is a comparison case but not directly used. The longcall strategy is more of a CONCENTRATED active strategy on specific names rather than a broad systematic factor harvest.

For our broader portfolio construction (and the curriculum's pedagogical goal), factor investing is the systematic implementation of behavioral premia. It's the structured, low-effort alternative to picking individual stocks. The cost is being correlated with broad markets in crises; the benefit is diversified factor exposure without active management.

A reasonable retail position: hold 60-80% of equity allocation in broad index funds (SPY, VTI), plus 20-40% in multi-factor ETFs to capture documented premia. The factor tilt adds expected return without significantly increasing risk. Adding individual stock-picking (active strategies, including our paper-trading-longcall) on top of this base is a further marginal-utility decision.

## The honest verdict

Factor investing offers:
- Real (though compressed) risk-adjusted return premium over cap-weighted benchmarks.
- Systematic, low-cost implementation.
- Diversification across multiple validated premia.

It does NOT offer:
- Beat-the-market magic.
- Protection from systematic equity drawdowns.
- Easy outperformance that justifies extreme tilts.

For most retail investors who want active exposure without active management complexity, multi-factor ETFs at 20-40% of equity allocation is a sound default. For sophisticated investors with edge in stock-picking or in specific strategies, factor investing is one of several inputs to portfolio construction, not the dominant driver.
