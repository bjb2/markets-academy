## Anchoring

The clean experimental demonstration (Tversky & Kahneman 1974):

> Participants spun a wheel of fortune that displayed either 10 or 65 (rigged; the wheel only stopped at those two values). Then they were asked: "Is the percentage of African countries in the UN higher or lower than [10 or 65]? What's your best estimate?"
>
> Median estimate for the 10-anchor group: 25%.
> Median estimate for the 65-anchor group: 45%.

The arbitrary spin of a wheel moved final estimates by 20 percentage points. Subjects were explicitly told the anchor was random and irrelevant. They knew it was random. They still anchored.

In market contexts, the anchors are usually NOT explicitly random. They're recent prices, IPO prices, your purchase price, 52-week highs, round numbers. Each acts as an anchor for "what this stock is worth," even when it isn't.

Specific manifestations:

- **The 52-week high anchor**: stocks near their 52-week high tend to underperform in the next month, partly because traders treat the prior high as a "ceiling" and sell into it. Stocks far from highs underperform because traders anchor "lost money" mentally.
- **Round-number anchors**: SPY trades through $400 with elevated volume; the round number is a psychological anchor that creates support/resistance.
- **Your purchase price**: the "I'll sell when it gets back to my cost" reflex is anchoring's most expensive form. Your cost basis is irrelevant to whether the stock is currently undervalued; your decision should depend only on current value vs current price.

Defense: explicitly anchor on FUNDAMENTAL value. A DCF (Layer 2.3) or multi-multiples estimate gives a price range that's independent of recent quotes. Compare the current price to that range, not to recent prices.

## Availability

The ease-of-recall heuristic: events that are easier to recall feel more frequent. Combined with media salience, this produces predictable overreactions to vivid events.

Empirical demonstrations:

- After a plane crash, people overestimate the probability of further crashes for weeks. Insurance sales for flight-cancellation insurance spike.
- After a corporate accounting fraud is revealed, retail flow OUT of the stock peaks days later, well after the institutional reaction; retail availability of the fraud headline drives delayed selling.
- After a hot IPO doubles on Day 1, retail interest in IPOs more broadly rises for months; the salient success makes "IPOs" feel like an asset class that wins.

A useful mental check: "How easily can I recall examples of [X]? Is that ease a true reflection of [X]'s frequency, or is it being driven by media salience, recency, vividness, or selective survivorship?"

For markets specifically:
- The names that come to mind quickly are the recent winners and the recent dramatic losers. The boring middle is missing.
- The strategies that sound like they "work" are the ones featured in books and interviews. The ones that quietly failed don't have books. Survivorship bias is availability + selection.

## Representativeness

Representativeness: judging probability by similarity to a stereotype, ignoring base rates.

The classical demonstration (Kahneman-Tversky 1972):

> "Bob is shy, withdrawn, helpful, but with little interest in people, or in the world of reality. A meek and tidy soul, he has a need for order and structure, and a passion for detail."
>
> What is Bob's profession? Subjects ranked librarian as more probable than farmer, even though there are 20x more US farmers than librarians.

Base rates exist; representativeness ignores them.

In markets:

- **Glamour stocks**: companies that "look like" past growth winners (FANG, NVDA pre-2024) get bid up because they fit the template. Most "next Apple" candidates aren't.
- **Story stocks**: a compelling narrative (cancer cure, EV transition, AI dominance) overrides base-rate skepticism. The narrative makes the bet feel more probable than base rates support.
- **Cheap stocks**: companies that "look like" past value traps (declining retailer, distressed energy producer) get sold below fundamental value because they fit the loser template. Some are real value; most are correctly priced.

The Fama-French value premium (Layer 3.5) is in part the market correcting representativeness biases. Cheap stocks that LOOK bad but are actually fine outperform glamour stocks that LOOK great but are actually overvalued. The mechanism: representativeness pushes prices to extremes, mean reversion (slowly, painfully) does the correction.

## Three additional biases worth naming

The 1974 paper started a cottage industry. A few more biases that are most relevant for trading:

- **Confirmation bias**: actively seek information that confirms your existing view; dismiss disconfirming evidence. Crippling for trading because it leads to position-sizing on false confidence and reluctance to exit losers.
- **Hindsight bias**: after an event happens, believe you "saw it coming" with more clarity than you actually had. Distorts post-mortems; makes trade-journal entries unreliable.
- **Endowment effect**: things you own feel more valuable than the same things you don't own. Makes you reluctant to sell positions you should sell.

Each becomes a tradable pattern when other traders are exhibiting it predictably. Most are also operating in YOU. The asymmetry of dealing with your own biases vs counterpartys' biases is the central tension of behavioral trading.

## The defense isn't being unbiased

A common misreading: "Now that I know about these biases, I'll be careful and avoid them." Empirically, this rarely works. The biases survive awareness.

The defense is STRUCTURAL: external commitments, rules, checklists, and pre-commitments that prevent System 1 from acting unchecked. Examples:

- Pre-trade checklist forcing System 2 evaluation before each new position.
- Position-sizing rules that don't depend on conviction (because conviction is biased).
- Stop-loss rules that exit independent of "I think it'll come back."
- A trade journal with explicit pre-trade hypotheses, evaluated after the fact.

The bias doesn't go away. The COSTS of the bias go down because System 2 has a chance to interrupt before action.
