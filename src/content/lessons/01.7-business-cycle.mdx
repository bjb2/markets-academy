## The cycle that organizes everything

The business cycle is the alternation of expansion and contraction in real economic activity (real GDP, employment, industrial production). The official US arbiter is the NBER, which dates recessions in retrospect, usually 6–12 months after they start.

A stylized cycle has four phases:

- **Expansion**: GDP rising, unemployment falling, capacity utilization climbing. Most years.
- **Late cycle**: Growth still positive but capacity tight, inflation pressures, central bank tightening. Equity multiples often peak here.
- **Recession**: GDP contracting (2 quarters is the rough heuristic, though NBER uses a broader basket). Unemployment rising.
- **Recovery / early cycle**: Activity bottoming, unemployment peaking, Fed easing. Strongest equity returns historically.

This is a description, not a clock. Cycles vary in length and severity. The 2009–2020 expansion was the longest on record. The 2020 recession was the shortest. Cycle phase identification in real time is harder than retrospective charts suggest.

## What leads, coincides, and lags

The Conference Board's Leading Economic Index (LEI) bundles indicators that tend to turn first:

- New manufacturing orders (especially capital goods)
- Building permits / housing starts
- Yield curve (covered last lesson)
- Initial jobless claims
- Consumer expectations surveys
- Equity market

**Coincident**: nonfarm payrolls, personal income, industrial production, real retail sales.

**Lagging**: unemployment rate, inflation, average duration of unemployment, business loan rates.

Two practical implications:

1. The unemployment rate is a *lagging* indicator. If you wait for it to rise to call a recession, you're already deep in one.
2. The most-reported numbers (unemployment, CPI) are not the early-warning ones. The early-warning ones are less famous (LEI, ISM, initial claims).

## What this means for investing

Cycle-aware investors do not try to time the cycle precisely, that requires forecasting that few have demonstrated reliably. Instead they:

- Shift allocation gradually toward defensives (utilities, staples, healthcare) when late-cycle signals stack up.
- Hold cash through late cycle to deploy in the recovery.
- Avoid leveraging into the late cycle.
- Recognize that early-cycle is when fortunes are made, by people who had cash and conviction.
