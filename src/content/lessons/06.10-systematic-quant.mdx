## The fundamental promise of systematic

A systematic trader writes down a strategy as RULES (often in code), backtests them on historical data, and follows them mechanically in live trading. The discipline replaces:
- "Should I buy here?" with "Does the signal fire?"
- "Is this scary?" with "Does the stop trigger?"
- "How much should I bet?" with "What's the position-sizing rule?"

The cleanest systematic frameworks express the entire strategy in mathematical terms. The behavior is COMPLETELY DETERMINISTIC given the input data: same data → same trade. No judgment, no improvisation, no panic.

## Carver's framework: <em>Systematic Trading</em>

Robert Carver (former AHL portfolio manager, author of <em>Systematic Trading</em> and <em>Leveraged Trading</em>) provides the most accessible practitioner framework for retail systematic trading.

The core principles:

### 1. Forecast quality > strategy complexity

A simple rule with good forecast quality beats a complex rule with poor forecast quality. Most strategy complexity is overfit; simple rules with proven generalizability survive longer.

Carver's preferred forecasts:
- Trend following (current price vs N-day moving average; works as time-series momentum)
- Carry (yield differential; works for FX, bonds, commodities)
- Mean reversion (overshoots reverting)
- Cross-instrument relative value (one asset vs another)

Each forecast generates a NORMALIZED SIGNAL (-1 to +1 typically). Position size scales with signal strength.

### 2. Diversification across instruments AND strategies

A single trend-following rule across 50 futures markets has more diversification than 50 different strategies in one market. The Carver framework recommends:
- 30-100 instruments (futures, FX pairs, equity indexes)
- 4-8 distinct strategy types (different forecasts)
- Combine via weighted averaging of signals

The result: a portfolio with hundreds of marginal positions, each small, where any single bad trade is irrelevant and the strategy-level performance smooths out via diversification.

### 3. Volatility targeting

The position size for each instrument scales with the INVERSE of its volatility. A high-vol commodity (crude oil) gets smaller size than a low-vol bond. Target portfolio volatility (typically 20-30% annualized) drives the total leverage.

Vol targeting has two benefits:
- Each position contributes roughly equal risk (mathematical diversification).
- Portfolio vol stays roughly constant through market regimes (the natural defense against vol expansion is reduced size).

### 4. Realistic expectations

Carver explicitly cautions against expecting institutional-grade Sharpe ratios. Achievable retail-scale systematic Sharpe: 0.4-0.8 across a multi-instrument multi-strategy framework, after costs. Single-instrument single-strategy implementations rarely exceed 0.3 net.

## How systematic differs from discretionary

| Dimension | Discretionary | Systematic |
|---|---|---|
| Decision basis | Judgment + experience | Quantitative rules |
| Backtest | Hard | Easy and necessary |
| Capacity | Manageable across few names | Scales across hundreds of instruments |
| Behavioral robustness | Vulnerable to emotion | Insulated by rules |
| Adaptability | High (responds to novel events) | Low (rules don't update) |
| Falsifiability | Hard (was the bad trade a mistake or unlucky?) | Clear (rule fired, outcome is known) |
| Execution speed | Slow (deliberation) | Fast (no decision required) |

Neither is inherently superior. The choice depends on:
- Your constitutional fit (do you panic in drawdowns? systematic is your friend).
- Your edge source (qualitative judgment vs rule-finding).
- Your time availability (systematic requires upfront work but light maintenance; discretionary requires constant attention).
- Your scale (systematic scales naturally; discretionary requires more time per dollar at scale).

## A simple systematic example

Carver's <em>Leveraged Trading</em> book walks through a starter system. Simplified:

1. **Instrument universe**: 5-10 liquid futures (S&P 500, Treasuries, gold, crude oil, copper, soy).
2. **Forecasts**: 64-day trend (current price - 64-day MA, normalized by vol), 16-day trend, 32-day carry. Three signals per instrument.
3. **Combined signal**: weighted average of three signals, capped at ±2.
4. **Position size**: signal × vol target / instrument vol. Higher signal AND lower-vol instrument = larger position.
5. **Rebalance**: weekly, or when signal changes meaningfully.

This implements the four Carver principles. Backtests on this simple structure produce Sharpe 0.6-0.9 across the 1990-2024 window. Real-world implementation incurs costs that may push Sharpe to 0.4-0.6. Not magic; honest.

## Common systematic strategy archetypes

The Carver framework's diversified portfolio typically includes:

1. **Trend following**: time-series momentum on futures (lesson 6.5).
2. **Carry**: yield-differential trades (lesson 6.7).
3. **Cross-sectional momentum**: long-short equity momentum (lesson 6.5).
4. **Volatility carry**: short volatility, long realized (the VIX risk premium).
5. **Mean reversion**: short-horizon reversal trades (lesson 6.6).
6. **Statistical arbitrage**: paired trades on correlated assets (lesson 6.6).

Each has been validated across decades. Each has periods of underperformance. Combining 4-8 of them via Carver-style diversification produces meaningful long-run Sharpe (0.6-1.0) net of costs in retail-accessible implementations.

## Modern systematic firms

For context: the institutional systematic landscape includes:

- **Two Sigma, Renaissance, DE Shaw, Citadel**: massive multi-strategy systematic firms with proprietary research and infrastructure. Returns reported in the 15-30% range historically, with Sharpe ratios 1.5+.
- **AQR, Man AHL, Winton, Aspect**: factor and trend-following systematic firms. More accessible to institutional investors via fund products. Returns more modest (8-15%) but with strong diversification.
- **CMT funds and managed futures**: trend-following on futures. Mixed performance across the post-2010 era.

Retail systematic is a small-scale version of this. The principles are the same; the infrastructure is much smaller.

## Where systematic fits

For our paper-trading-longcall project, we're effectively a systematic strategy: rules-based entry, position sizing, exit. The framework's strength is consistent execution. Its weakness is rigidity in novel circumstances.

For broader portfolio construction:
- 80% broad index funds (passive)
- 10-15% multi-factor ETFs (systematic factor harvest)
- 5-10% individual stocks or active strategies (paper-trading-longcall)

This is a systematic approach to total portfolio construction. The active component is a small (~10%) allocation that adds optionality without overwhelming the long-term compounding from the passive base.
