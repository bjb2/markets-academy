## A definition that actually works

Money is not a thing. Money is a *credit instrument* — a record of who owes whom, denominated in a common unit. The thing in your wallet (a dollar bill) is one specific kind of money-token: a non-interest-bearing IOU issued by the Federal Reserve. The number in your checking account is a different kind: an IOU from your bank, denominated in Fed money. The savings bond in a drawer is yet another: an IOU from the US Treasury, denominated in Fed money.

These distinctions matter because not all money is created equal. **Perry Mehrling's hierarchy of money** is the single best frame:

```
Gold (or external standard) — top of hierarchy
        ↓ promises to pay
Central bank money (Fed liabilities — base money)
        ↓ promises to pay
Commercial bank money (deposits — most of M1/M2)
        ↓ promises to pay
Securities (Treasuries, MBS, corporate paper)
        ↓ promises to pay
Other private credit (loans, IOUs)
```

Each level is a promise to pay the level above. In a crisis, promises further down get questioned first. "Cash is king" really means "Fed liabilities suddenly trade at a premium because everyone wants to climb the hierarchy."

## The monetary aggregates

The Fed publishes a few measures of "the money supply":

- **MB / base money**: physical currency + bank reserves at the Fed.
- **M1**: base + checking deposits (the stuff you actually spend).
- **M2**: M1 + savings deposits + small time deposits (mostly bank money).
- **M3 / broader**: was discontinued in 2006; was M2 + institutional money funds.

For most analytical purposes M2 is the right number. It's what people refer to when they say "the money supply." Track it at FRED (series M2SL).

## Why "money printing" is a bad mental model

When the Fed buys Treasuries (QE), it creates base money — but base money mostly sits as bank reserves at the Fed. Whether that *becomes* broad money (M2) and propagates into prices depends on whether banks lend it out, which depends on demand, regulation, and capital constraints. The 2009–2019 QE programs expanded base money dramatically without producing equivalent M2 growth or CPI inflation. The 2020–2021 expansion did show up in M2 — because that round was paired with direct fiscal transfers, not just bank reserve creation.

The lesson: "the Fed prints money" is shorthand that often misleads. The right question is "is broad money expanding, and what regime is the velocity in."
