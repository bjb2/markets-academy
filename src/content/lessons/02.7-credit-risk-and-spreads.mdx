## Credit spread = corporate yield minus Treasury yield

`Credit spread = Corporate YTM - Treasury YTM (same maturity)`

It's quoted in basis points (1 bp = 0.01%). A "150 bp spread" means the corporate yields 150 bp more than the matching-maturity Treasury.

The spread compensates the bondholder for three things:

1. **Expected default loss**: probability of default × loss given default.
2. **Liquidity premium**: corporate bonds are less liquid than Treasuries; investors demand extra yield to bear that.
3. **Risk premium**: even after expected loss is priced, investors don't want to bear default risk for free; they demand a risk premium on top.

For investment-grade bonds, expected default loss is small (basis points) and the spread is mostly liquidity + risk premium. For high-yield ("junk") bonds, expected default loss is the largest term.

## Credit rating ladders

Two main rating agencies dominate US fixed income: **Moody's** and **S&P**. Their ratings:

| Moody's | S&P | Category | Default risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aaa | AAA | Highest investment grade | Near zero (US Treasury, a few corporates) |
| Aa | AA | High investment grade | Very low |
| A | A | Upper investment grade | Low |
| Baa | BBB | Lower investment grade | Moderate |
| Ba | BB | Upper high yield | Elevated |
| B | B | High yield | High |
| Caa-C | CCC-C | Distressed | Very high |
| D | D | In default | 100% |

The boundary between BBB and BB is the boundary between **investment grade (IG)** and **high yield (HY) / junk**. This boundary matters because many institutional buyers (pensions, insurance, money funds) are restricted to IG; a downgrade across the line ("fallen angel") triggers FORCED SELLING and amplifies the price move.

Historical default rates over 5 years (Moody's data, multi-decade averages):
- AAA: ~0.1%
- AA: ~0.5%
- A: ~1.5%
- BBB: ~3%
- BB: ~10%
- B: ~25%
- Caa-C: ~50%+

These are CUMULATIVE 5-year defaults, not annual. The point is the curvature: defaults rise faster than linearly as you descend the ladder.

## The credit cycle

Credit spreads are not constant. They oscillate with the economy, the Fed, and investor sentiment. <SourceLink id="marks-memos-best-of" /> documents this cycle obsessively; the canonical Marks framing:

| Phase | Spread behavior | Investor behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Expansion** | Tight (near historical lows) | Reaching for yield; covenant-lite issuance; record-low spreads |
| **Late-cycle** | Compressed but starting to widen on specific names | Discriminating among credits begins |
| **Crisis / recession** | Wide (often 2-4x normal) | Forced selling; correlations spike; fallen-angel cascades |
| **Recovery** | Wide but stable, then tightening | Patient capital deployed at high spreads; future-good-vintage |

Marks' "the buy of a lifetime" episodes correspond to the recession-trough widening: 2002 (telecom bust), 2008-09 (GFC), 2020 (COVID). Each saw HY spreads top 1,000 bp; each was followed by extraordinary returns for buyers brave enough to deploy.

In late-cycle expansions (2007, 2019, late 2021), spreads compress to record-low levels and risk premia disappear. Buying at these moments produces poor risk-adjusted returns: you bear elevated default risk for minimal extra yield.

## Spread products in practice

The corporate bond universe is roughly:

- **Investment grade (IG)** corporate: $7T+ in US alone. Issuers like AAPL, MSFT, JPM, KO. Spreads typically 50-200 bp.
- **High yield (HY)** corporate: $1.5T+. Issuers like Carnival, Avis, energy producers, retailers. Spreads typically 300-700 bp in normal markets, 1,000+ bp in crises.
- **Emerging market sovereign**: $4T+. Hard-currency or local-currency. Spreads from 100 bp (Korea) to 1,000+ bp (Argentina, Egypt).
- **Leveraged loans**: $1.5T+. Floating-rate bank loans to leveraged-buyout companies. Different mechanics from bonds (floating rate, senior secured) but same credit-risk principles.
- **Structured credit**: ABS, CLO, CMBS, RMBS. Pools of mortgages, auto loans, corporate loans, commercial mortgages, packaged into tranches with seniority rules.

Each market has its own conventions, liquidity profile, and idiosyncratic risks, but all rest on the basic spread-over-Treasury construction.

## Why spread is a real-time recession signal

Credit spreads update every minute. Most other macro signals (GDP, employment, even most yield-curve calculations) update with lags. Spreads move on every news item that adjusts default expectations.

In particular:
- Industry-specific bad news (e.g., regional bank stress in March 2023) shows up immediately as widening in those credits.
- Generalized risk-off shows up as broad spread widening.
- "Fallen angel" events (BBB downgraded to BB) trigger forced institutional selling and visible spread spikes.

Tracking HY OAS (option-adjusted spread, the ICE BofA HY index is the standard) is a low-cost real-time recession watch. Sustained breach of 600 bp from a sub-400 bp regime is a strong warning.

## What this means for our work

For the paper-trading-longcall project, credit considerations don't directly apply (we trade equity options). But the credit cycle frames the EQUITY environment:

- When HY spreads compress to record-lows, equity valuations are typically also stretched and forward equity returns are below average.
- When HY spreads widen sharply, equity has often (not always) followed with a drawdown.
- The 2020 cycle saw both move together: HY spreads spiked to 1,100 bp and equities dropped 35% in March; HY tightened back below 400 bp and equities ran +100% over the next 18 months.

Spread monitoring is an institutional discipline retail investors typically skip but that genuinely improves cycle awareness.
