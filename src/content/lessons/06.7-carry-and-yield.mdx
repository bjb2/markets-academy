## What carry means

The concept generalizes beyond FX. Carry is the income generated by holding an asset, NET of the cost of financing the position. Examples:

| Asset class | Carry | Example |
|---|---|---|
| FX | Interest rate differential | Long AUD, short JPY |
| Government bonds | Yield to maturity minus financing cost | Long 10-yr Treasury, financed at fed funds |
| Equity index futures | Dividend yield minus financing cost | Long SPX futures, financed at SOFR |
| Commodity futures | Contango/backwardation | Roll yield from rolling expiring contracts |
| Credit (corporate bonds) | Credit spread over risk-free | Long IG bond, financed at Treasury |

In each case, you earn (or pay) something for HOLDING the position, separate from price changes. Carry is the structural income; the alpha question is whether the carry compensates for the risk.

## The carry trade in detail

The empirical "Asness, Moskowitz, Pedersen (AMP) 2013" paper (Value and Momentum Everywhere) and earlier work documented:

- **FX carry**: long-run Sharpe of buying high-yield currencies and shorting low-yield: ~0.4-0.6 over decades. Premium has compressed somewhat post-2008.
- **Bond carry**: rolling a steep yield curve (longer-maturity bonds yield more than short-maturity), holding for the yield differential. Sharpe ~0.3-0.5.
- **Commodity carry**: structural backwardation/contango produces a roll return regardless of spot price. Specific commodities (e.g., crude oil, copper) have varied carry regimes.
- **Equity carry**: dividend yield minus financing cost. Smaller absolute return but stable.

Carry is the EQUIVALENT of value (lessons 6.1-6.4) for cross-asset trades. Both involve buying something cheap (high-yielding) and selling something expensive (low-yielding) in some reference dimension. The economic logic: investors demand a return premium for bearing the relevant risk (currency risk, duration risk, default risk).

## Why carry works most of the time

Three explanations:

### 1. Interest rate differentials reflect fundamental factors

A country yielding 4% likely has higher inflation, weaker reserves, or higher default risk than a country yielding 0.1%. The yield differential compensates investors for bearing these risks. Most of the time (calm markets, normal regimes), the risks don't materialize and the carry is collected.

### 2. Forward rate puzzle

Under "covered interest parity," forward exchange rates should equal current spot rates adjusted by the interest rate differential. Empirically, forward rates are POOR PREDICTORS of actual future spot rates. The forward rate "predicts" depreciation that doesn't fully materialize on average. This is the basis of carry trade profitability: actual currency moves don't fully offset the rate differential.

### 3. Behavioral and structural premiums

Some carry premia reflect rational risk premia (defaults, sovereign stress). Others reflect behavioral preferences (yield-seeking, lottery-like alternatives, governance/structural restrictions on capital allocation). The mix varies by asset class and regime.

## Why carry collapses periodically

The carry trade's signature feature is NEGATIVE SKEW: most periods deliver small positive returns, occasional periods deliver catastrophic losses. The mechanism:

1. Carry trades are sized based on calm-period volatility.
2. A risk-off shock causes investors to LIQUIDATE risky positions simultaneously.
3. The "low-yield safe haven" currencies (JPY, CHF, USD) appreciate sharply as capital flows in.
4. The "high-yield risky" currencies (AUD, BRL, ZAR, TRY) depreciate sharply as capital flows out.
5. Carry traders' simultaneous unwinds amplify the moves.
6. Multi-week positions that were carrying 1-2% per month can lose 15-25% in a few days.

Famous events:
- **August 2007**: subprime credit panic; AUD/JPY fell 10% in days.
- **October 2008**: Lehman collapse; AUD/JPY fell 30% in weeks.
- **August 2015**: China currency devaluation triggered global carry unwind.
- **March 2020**: COVID risk-off; multiple carry trades collapsed simultaneously.
- **August 2024**: JPY appreciated sharply on hawkish BOJ shift; carry trades unwound globally.

Each event followed the same script. The trade works for years between events; events wipe out years of accumulated gains in days.

## How to access carry as a retail investor

Three practical paths:

### 1. FX carry ETFs

DBV (Invesco DB G10 Currency Harvest) and similar funds hold a basket of long high-yield / short low-yield currency exposures. Expense ratios 0.55-0.75%. Have produced moderate Sharpe over the long run with the expected drawdown profile.

### 2. Emerging market sovereign bonds

EMB (iShares JPMorgan USD Emerging Markets Bond ETF) and similar hold dollar-denominated sovereign EM bonds. Yields 5-7% above US Treasuries. Carries the carry premium plus the credit/default premium. Significant duration risk too. Expense ratios 0.40-0.60%.

### 3. Corporate bond carry

LQD (IG corporate bonds) and HYG (high-yield corporate bonds) provide credit-spread carry over Treasuries. IG carries ~100-150 bp over Treasuries; HY carries 300-500+ bp. Default risk and duration risk both elevated vs Treasuries. Liquidity risk in stress periods (March 2020 LQD-NAV-gap was real).

For our paper-trading-longcall project, carry isn't directly applicable to the options sleeve. But the framework is useful: an option's TIME PREMIUM is a form of "negative carry" you pay each day. Selling options harvests this carry; buying options pays it. The strategy choices (covered call, short-strangle, long-call, long-put) each have different carry profiles.

## Carry's place in a diversified portfolio

Carry strategies generate returns that are often UNCORRELATED with broader equity returns in calm periods, and CORRELATED in crisis periods (the "diversification works until you need it" pattern from lesson 3.2).

A diversified active portfolio:
- Value + carry (cross-asset value): captures structural premia
- Momentum + trend: rides behavioral underreaction
- Mean reversion: captures order-flow inefficiencies and valuation cycles

Adding carry to a value-momentum portfolio typically improves long-run Sharpe by 10-20% in calm markets; the crisis-correlation property means it doesn't fully de-correlate in panics. AMP 2013 documented this combination as the foundation of "factor investing" beyond equities.
