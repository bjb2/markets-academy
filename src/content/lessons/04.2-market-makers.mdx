## What a market maker does

A market maker (MM) posts simultaneous bids and asks on a security throughout the trading day. Examples:

- **Designated MMs**: each NYSE-listed stock has a Designated Market Maker (formerly "specialist") with explicit obligations to maintain a continuous two-sided market.
- **Electronic MMs**: firms like Citadel Securities, Virtu, Jane Street, Susquehanna run automated quoting in tens of thousands of securities simultaneously. Equities, options, ETFs, futures, crypto.
- **Wholesalers**: a subset of electronic MMs that PAY brokers for retail order flow (payment for order flow, lesson 4.5), then internalize those orders against their own inventory.

A typical day for an electronic MM:
- Quote, say, AAPL with a 1¢ spread, 500 shares on each side, throughout the day.
- Get hit on the bid (someone sold to them at 100.01); now LONG 500 shares.
- A second later, someone lifts the offer (buys from them at 100.02); now FLAT, with 1¢ × 500 = $5 profit.
- Repeat thousands of times per second across thousands of names.

The aggregate edge per round trip is tiny. The aggregate VOLUME is enormous. Virtu reported 1,485 winning days out of 1,485 trading days in its first IPO-era window, with daily revenues averaging hundreds of thousands of dollars from spreads.

## Why a market maker can profit even on a small spread

Two things make the small-spread game viable at scale:

1. **Volume**: a 1¢ edge × 10,000 trades/day × 500 shares × 0.5 fill rate ≈ $25,000/day per name. At 1,000 names that's $25M/day. (Numbers illustrative; real figures depend on inventory turnover and fill probability.)
2. **Adverse selection management**: most retail and small-institutional flow is uninformed (no information edge). Most of the time, the trader sells because they need cash or have a non-information motive. Such flow is profitable for MMs. The hard cases are INFORMED flow: someone who knows something. MMs use models to detect this and widen quotes (or pull them entirely) when adverse selection signals fire.

This is the core insight of the Glosten-Milgrom model (1985): the bid-ask spread is the MM's COMPENSATION FOR ADVERSE SELECTION RISK plus inventory cost. The spread isn't a tax; it's a price for a service rendered (immediacy) and a hedge against being picked off.

## The Stoll-Ho inventory framework

A second classic model (Stoll 1978, Ho-Stoll 1981): MMs manage inventory, not just spreads. Holding 50,000 shares of AAPL exposes the MM to AAPL price risk. They want to be FLAT (zero net position) at end of day.

The mechanism: when inventory grows long, MM LOWERS its bid to discourage more selling and lowers its ask to encourage more buying. Quotes drift in the direction that returns inventory toward zero. This is sometimes called "skewing quotes."

A useful observation: if you watch the bid and ask move while the mid stays constant, that's typically inventory management, not opinion. If you watch the mid move while the spread stays constant, that's typically opinion (or response to an external signal).

## Adverse selection in action

The classic example: a stock is quoted 100.00 bid / 100.05 ask. A large news event (earnings beat) is about to hit. An informed trader (or algo) sees the news milliseconds before the MM and lifts the offer for 10,000 shares at 100.05.

Two seconds later, the news is public, and the stock is trading at 100.50. The MM that sold at 100.05 is now SHORT 10,000 shares at a $45,000 loss.

This is "getting picked off" or "adverse selection." Three mitigations:

1. **Wider spreads around scheduled events**: MMs quote with bigger gaps near earnings, FOMC, NFP releases.
2. **Order-type analytics**: large aggressive orders from unusual sources are flagged as potentially informed; quotes pull.
3. **Latency arbitrage**: faster MMs see news feeds and update quotes BEFORE slower MMs can be picked off. This is one driver of the HFT arms race (lesson 4.6).

For retail traders, the practical takeaway: trading right around scheduled news releases means trading against widened spreads (cost) AND high adverse selection risk (volatility). Most retail strategies should avoid the windows immediately surrounding scheduled announcements.

## Where MM profits show up vs vanish

MMs make money in:

- High-volume, normal-volatility markets where flow is mostly uninformed.
- Names with consistent two-way order flow.
- Calm sessions where inventory can be flattened easily.

MMs lose money in:

- Surprise news cascades (one-sided flow from informed traders).
- Trending markets where inventory accumulates one direction faster than it can be unwound.
- Sudden volatility expansion (option MMs get hit hard when realized vol >> implied).

The 2010 "Flash Crash" was partially an inventory crisis: market makers' algorithms detected unusual flow, widened their quotes drastically (or pulled entirely), and the resulting liquidity vacuum allowed prices to gap. Some MMs lost a great deal of money very quickly when they couldn't reduce inventory at any reasonable price.
