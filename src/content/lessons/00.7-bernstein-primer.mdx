## The 27-page detour that does most of the work

William Bernstein's *If You Can* is 27 pages, free, and contains roughly half of all sensible personal-finance advice ever written. It is structured around five "hurdles" any investor must clear. We'll cover the structure here and then send you to read the original.

Get the PDF: <SourceLink id="bernstein-if-you-can" />. It is genuinely short. Read it cover to cover.

## The five hurdles (Bernstein's frame)

1. **Save enough.** Bernstein argues for ~15–20% of gross income, invested early and consistently. No fancy strategy can rescue a low savings rate. No fancy strategy needs to overcome a high one.
2. **Understand finance basics.** What stocks and bonds are. What the market is. What inflation does. (Layers 0–2 of this curriculum are the same content, expanded.)
3. **Learn the history.** Markets crash periodically. People who lived through 1973–74, 2000–02, 2008–09 know this in their bones. People who did not, panic-sell during the next one. Reading market history is the cheapest substitute for living through it.
4. **Know thyself.** Most investing failures are behavioral, not analytical. The biggest enemy is the person in the mirror at 3am during a 30% drawdown. Build a portfolio you can hold through *that* moment, not through good times.
5. **Recognize the industry's incentives.** Most of the financial industry is structured to extract fees from you. Their interests and yours are not aligned. Bernstein is blunt: assume any advisor charging a percentage of assets is your adversary until proven otherwise.

## Where Bernstein is right that most curricula get wrong

The biggest insight is the ordering. Save first, then learn. Most people invert this. They try to learn enough to stock-pick their way out of an inadequate savings rate, which is mathematically impossible at any non-trivial confidence level. Bernstein's five-hurdle order is the actual difficulty progression: the easy stuff first (save), the hard stuff last (don't sabotage yourself).
