## A concrete moment

In May 2024 the spot price of copper rose roughly 20% over six weeks with no single dominant news story. Some traders attributed it to AI-driven electrical infrastructure demand. Others to a short squeeze on COMEX. Others to Chinese smelter cuts. The truth was probably some of each.

Notice what *you* didn't have to do to act on it: you didn't have to know which story was right. The price had already factored in the marginal trader's read of all of them. If you used copper in your business, you rebudgeted. If you were considering a copper mine investment, the price changed your math. The coordination happened — without a central planner ever assembling those facts.

## The general claim

This is Hayek's argument: a price is not a transaction record. It is the densest possible summary of distributed knowledge — facts no one person could ever assemble — compressed into one number.

## Why this comes first

Every later layer is mechanical without this frame. If you do not believe that prices aggregate information you cannot otherwise access, you will spend your career trying to second-guess them from your armchair. If you do believe it, you become humble about your edge, suspicious of your forecasts, and curious about *why* a price moved rather than confident about *where* it should be.

A practical consequence: every time you find yourself thinking "this stock is obviously mispriced," the first move is not to trade. The first move is to ask what the market knows that you don't. Sometimes the answer is "nothing — they're wrong." Most of the time the answer is "they know something I'm about to learn an expensive lesson about."

## What prices are NOT

Prices are not fair. They are not just. They are not the right number. They are a clearing point — the place where the marginal buyer and marginal seller meet. If you find that point morally distasteful (rent is too high, oil is too cheap, AI engineers earn too much) you may be right about the morality, but you are confused about what the price is doing.

This distinction matters because most market commentary collapses it. "The market is overvalued" is a moral claim disguised as an analytical one. "The marginal buyer is paying 28× earnings, which historically has implied 4–5% forward annual returns" is the same content, restated as a price relationship. The second is useful.
