## The claim

A price is not just a transaction record. It is the densest possible summary of distributed knowledge — millions of facts that no one person could ever assemble — compressed into one number.

When the price of copper rises, you do not need to know whether it rose because a mine flooded in Chile, demand surged from Chinese construction, a new substitute material failed in testing, or a hedge fund bought a forward contract. You only need to act on the number. Everyone who uses copper rebudgets. Everyone who could supply it considers entry. The price has done the coordination work for you, and it cost no central planner.

This is Hayek's argument in <SourceLink id="hayek-1945" />. Eight pages. Read it before the next lesson.

## Why this comes first

Every later layer is mechanical without this frame. If you do not believe that prices aggregate information you cannot otherwise access, you will spend your career trying to second-guess them from your armchair. If you do believe it, you become humble about your edge, suspicious of your forecasts, and curious about *why* a price moved rather than confident about *where* it should be.

A practical consequence: every time you find yourself thinking "this stock is obviously mispriced," the first move is not to trade. The first move is to ask what the market knows that you don't. Sometimes the answer is "nothing — they're wrong." Most of the time the answer is "they know I'm about to learn an expensive lesson."

## What prices are not

Prices are not fair. They are not just. They are not the right number. They are a clearing point — the place where the marginal buyer and marginal seller meet. If you find that point morally distasteful (rent is too high, oil is too cheap, AI engineers earn too much) you may be right about the morality, but you are confused about what the price is doing.

This distinction matters because most market commentary collapses it. "The market is overvalued" is a moral claim disguised as an analytical one. "The marginal buyer is paying 28× earnings, which historically has implied 4–5% forward annual returns" is the same content, restated as a price relationship. The second is useful.

## What you should be carrying out of this lesson

- A price is a compressed information signal, not a verdict.
- The market is doing coordination work you couldn't do yourself.
- "Mispriced" is the kind of claim that should make you suspicious of yourself first.
- Hayek's essay is the foundational text. Reread it once a year for the rest of your investing life.
