## Two ways to measure the same thing

Everything in finance has two versions: **nominal** and **real**. Nominal means "in dollars as printed." Real means "in dollars adjusted for inflation, so they buy the same basket of goods."

When you see "stocks returned 10% last year," the next question is whether that 10% is nominal or real. A 10% nominal return in a year with 8% inflation is a 2% real return. Compounded over 30 years, the difference between 10% nominal and 2% real is the difference between "comfortable retirement" and "ate the cat food in the end."

The formal relationship is the **Fisher equation**:

```
(1 + nominal) = (1 + real) × (1 + inflation)
```

## What inflation actually measures

In the US, the headline inflation number is **CPI** (Consumer Price Index), produced by the BLS. It's a price index for a fixed basket of consumer goods. There are variants: core CPI (ex food and energy, less noisy), PCE (the Fed's preferred measure, slightly different basket), trimmed-mean PCE (removes the most extreme components).

Track them yourself at <SourceLink id="fred-cpi" />. Every Fed decision is downstream of these series. Reading the latest BLS release before the financial press writes about it is a 30-minute habit that pays off forever.

## Why this matters for every market

- **Bonds**: a 5% Treasury yield with 4% inflation is a 1% real yield. The same yield with 2% inflation is a 3% real yield. The bond did not change; the world's claim on you did.
- **Stocks**: revenue and earnings are nominal. In high-inflation regimes, headline EPS growth flatters because input costs and wages haven't caught up yet, they will. Real EPS is the durable signal.
- **Cash**: cash always has a 0% real yield minus inflation. In 2022 cash earned roughly −8% real. The "safe" asset can be the most lethal one.
- **Wages and salaries**: most of your felt experience of "am I doing better" is real wage growth. Headline raises in inflationary periods feel hollow because they are.

## The discipline

When anyone gives you a return number, mentally tag it nominal or real. If they didn't say, assume nominal, and immediately calculate the real version. Most "outperformance" stories collapse when re-stated in real terms. Most "stocks for the long run" arguments are real-return arguments in disguise.
