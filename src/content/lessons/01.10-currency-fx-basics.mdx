## Why currencies move

An exchange rate is a relative price. EUR/USD = 1.10 means 1 euro buys 1.10 dollars. To predict where it goes, you predict the joint evolution of two economies, two central banks, and two political systems. That's hard.

A few mechanisms dominate FX over different horizons:

**Days to weeks: interest rate differentials.** Money flows toward the currency offering higher real yields, all else equal. When the Fed hikes more aggressively than the ECB, USD strengthens against EUR.

**Months to years: relative growth and inflation.** Stronger economies with credible inflation control attract capital. The "Dollar Smile" framework: USD strengthens both when the US grows strongly AND in global crises (flight to safety). It weakens when the rest of the world is outgrowing the US.

**Years to decades: purchasing-power parity (PPP) and current account dynamics.** Currencies eventually trade in a band related to relative price levels of tradable goods. Persistent current account deficits eventually pressure the currency. "Eventually" can take a decade.

## Carry trade — the simplest FX strategy

Borrow in a low-yielding currency, invest in a high-yielding currency, pocket the rate differential. Classic example: borrow JPY at 0.5%, hold AUD bonds at 4%. As long as the exchange rate doesn't move against you, you collect ~3.5% per year.

The carry trade has been one of the most-traded FX strategies for 30 years. It works for years and then unwinds violently when risk sentiment turns. The 2008 unwind of yen carry trades produced ~30% JPY appreciation in 6 months — wiping out years of carry profits in a quarter.

This is the deepest lesson in FX: high carry currencies look like free money until they're not. They're paying you a risk premium for tail risk that materializes infrequently but catastrophically. Most "uncorrelated" FX strategies turn out to be hidden short-vol bets.

## The dollar's special status

The US dollar is the global reserve currency. ~60% of global FX reserves, ~80% of trade finance, the denominator of most commodities. This produces unique features:

- Persistent demand from non-US actors needing dollar liquidity (foreign banks, EM corporates with USD debt).
- "Dollar smile" — USD strengthens in BOTH US-led booms AND global crises. Most currencies do one or the other; USD does both.
- Fed policy spills onto every other economy. A Fed hike tightens financial conditions globally because dollar borrowing costs everywhere rise.

Foreign currency exposure is not the obvious "diversifier" it seems. Most US investors' optimal currency exposure to non-USD is close to zero, because the dollar's safe-haven property already hedges what a foreign currency would supposedly hedge against.
