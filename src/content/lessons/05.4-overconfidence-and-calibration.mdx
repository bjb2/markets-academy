## Overconfidence in three flavors

The term covers three distinct phenomena, often conflated:

1. **Overprecision**: confidence intervals are too narrow. When you estimate a quantity and give "90% confidence," your interval actually captures the truth only 50-70% of the time. The interval is too tight; your sense of precision is wrong.

2. **Overestimation**: you think you'll do better than you actually will. Estimate of own future returns, estimate of own driving skill, estimate of own diet adherence. All systematically optimistic.

3. **Overplacement**: you think you're better than average. ~80% of drivers think they're above-average. ~70% of mutual fund managers think they're above-average. Mathematically impossible.

For traders, overprecision is the most expensive flavor. It manifests as: "I'm 90% sure this trade will work" → position sized at 10% of capital → fails 30-40% of the time, not 10%.

## Calibration: what good prediction-tracking looks like

A well-calibrated forecaster, asked to give probabilities on many events:
- When they say "70%", the events happen 70% of the time.
- When they say "50%", the events happen 50% of the time.
- The line of average outcome vs stated probability has slope 1.

A poorly-calibrated forecaster (most humans):
- When they say "90%", the events happen 70% of the time.
- When they say "50%", the events happen 45-55% of the time.
- The line is below the 45-degree calibration line, especially at high-confidence levels.

To MEASURE your own calibration, you need a tracking system. The standard tool is the BRIER SCORE.

## Brier scoring

Define: for each prediction, you stated probability p of the event happening. The actual outcome o is 0 or 1.

Brier score for one prediction = (p - o)².

For many predictions, average the scores. Lower = better calibrated.

- Perfect: 0 (every probability matches the actual outcome).
- Random: 0.25 (every probability is 0.5; outcomes are 50/50).
- Worst possible: 1 (every probability is 1.0 and outcome is 0, or vice versa).

A useful benchmark: weather forecasters have Brier scores around 0.10-0.15. Trained intelligence analysts: 0.13-0.18. Untrained pundits: 0.25-0.40 (slightly worse than random for some).

The mechanism that makes calibration improvable: explicit feedback. Once you score 100+ predictions, you can see WHERE you're miscalibrated and adjust. Pure intuition + no feedback = chronically miscalibrated; many predictions + tracking = improvement over months.

## Why overconfidence persists despite feedback

A natural question: people make trades, see outcomes, get years of feedback. Why doesn't overconfidence get corrected?

Several mechanisms keep it intact:

- **Selective recall**: winning trades are vividly remembered; losing trades blur. After 5 years, the trader's mental record is 70% wins, even though actual record is 50%.
- **Hindsight bias**: after an outcome, "I knew this would happen" is the default narrative. Forecasts that turned out wrong get re-remembered as having been uncertain.
- **Attribution asymmetry**: wins are attributed to skill, losses to bad luck or "unforeseen events." Skill estimate doesn't update on losses.
- **No comparison group**: you don't see the trades you DIDN'T make. The hypothetical "what if I'd not done X" is hard to evaluate, so the actual win rate is the only data point, and it's biased.

The defense: a TRADE JOURNAL. Pre-trade, write the thesis, target, stop, and assigned probability. Post-trade, score honestly: did the thesis play out? Was the probability assignment accurate? Score yourself with Brier. Over months, the data forces calibration even when intuition doesn't.

## Tetlock and Superforecasting

Philip Tetlock spent decades studying forecast accuracy. Headline findings from <em>Expert Political Judgment</em> (2005) and <em>Superforecasting</em> (2015):

1. Experts in political forecasting are about as accurate as well-informed laypeople. Pundits with strong narratives ("hedgehogs") were systematically WORSE than analysts who held multiple perspectives ("foxes").
2. A small subset of forecasters (the "superforecasters") consistently outperformed both experts and average citizens, with Brier scores ~30% better than competing forecasters.
3. Superforecasters share traits: probabilistic thinking, willingness to update on evidence, deliberate practice with feedback, comfortable with uncertainty.

The lesson for traders: the superforecaster traits are LEARNABLE. They're not innate. The path is journaling, scoring, and deliberate practice with calibration feedback.

## Specific trading manifestations

Three places overconfidence chronically costs money:

### 1. Position sizing

If you say "I'm 90% sure" and size accordingly, but you're actually 60%, your expected utility is materially negative. Position sizing should account for your CALIBRATED probability, not your felt probability. The "Kelly fraction" position size with miscalibrated probabilities is the canonical way to blow up an account.

### 2. Entries and exits

Holding losers because "I'm sure it'll come back" is overconfidence dressed as conviction. Selling winners because "I'm sure it's topped" is the same bias in the opposite direction. Both reflect false certainty about future direction.

### 3. Trade frequency

Overconfident traders trade more often. Each trade has a positive subjective expected return; the trader is sure this one will work. Empirically (Barber & Odean): retail traders' returns DECREASE with trade frequency, because frictions accumulate and the assumed alpha doesn't materialize. The most active retail accounts underperform the least active.

The single most reliable improvement for many retail traders: trade LESS. The calibrated cost of overconfidence + trading frictions usually exceeds the alpha being chased.

## Pre-mortems: a structural defense

A useful technique from Gary Klein and used in Superforecasting circles:

Before entering a trade or making a decision, ask:
- "Imagine it's 6 months from now and this position has lost money. WHY did it lose?"
- Generate 3-5 plausible reasons in writing.

This forces the mind to consider FAILURE MODES that overconfidence is suppressing. The pre-mortem makes counterfactuals concrete, which they aren't otherwise (the bias is "this will work because [my thesis]"). After the pre-mortem, position sizing often shrinks because the failure modes are now mentally available.

Cousin technique: write the THREE reasons you'd be wrong, and explicitly assign probability to each. If the assigned probability of being wrong exceeds 30-40%, sizing should be smaller than your initial impulse suggested.
