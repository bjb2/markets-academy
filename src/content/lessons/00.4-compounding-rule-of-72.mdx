## The exponential nobody respects

Linear growth: add a fixed amount every period. Exponential growth: multiply by a fixed amount every period. Humans have terrible intuition for exponential growth. We feel small differences in growth rate as small differences in outcome. They are not.

| Annual return | $10K after 30 years |
|---|---:|
| 4% | $32,434 |
| 7% | $76,123 |
| 10% | $174,494 |
| 13% | $389,757 |

The gap between 4% and 13% is not 3×. It is 12×. At 30 years, a 6-point spread in annual return is roughly an order of magnitude in terminal wealth.

This single fact is why fees matter, why starting early matters, why staying invested through drawdowns matters, and why almost every retirement-math piece of advice can be derived from it. Once you internalize compounding, you stop arguing about most personal-finance questions.

## The Rule of 72

A useful mental shortcut: **the number of years for money to double is approximately 72 ÷ annual return**.

- At 6%: 72/6 = 12 years to double.
- At 9%: 72/9 = 8 years to double.
- At 12%: 72/12 = 6 years to double.

The rule of 72 is an approximation of the exact formula `years = ln(2) / ln(1 + r)`. It is accurate within a few percent for typical rates (4–15%) and useful enough to do in your head while someone is pitching you.

## Why the math gets *less* respected with experience, not more

Most people accept compounding intellectually and then act inconsistently with it. They time the market, switch funds, chase performance, take advice from people who charge 1% of AUM annually. Every one of those behaviors is a tiny tax on the exponent. Over 30 years, tiny taxes on the exponent are catastrophic.

The discipline is not "understand compounding." The discipline is "do nothing for 30 years that taxes the exponent." The "do nothing" part is the hard part.

## Carrying forward

- Tiny differences in compounded return → enormous differences in terminal wealth.
- 72 ÷ rate ≈ years to double. Memorize.
- The trap isn't ignorance; it's behavior that drains the exponent.
