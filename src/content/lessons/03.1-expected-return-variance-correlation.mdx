## A concrete instance: SPY since the GFC

SPY's total return in calendar 2017 was about +21.7%. In 2018 it was about -4.6%. In 2019 it was about +31.2%. In 2020 it was about +18.3%. In 2022 it was about -18.2%. The mean of those five numbers is about +9.7%. The standard deviation is about 19.4%. That second number is what people mean when they say "stocks have 15-20% annual vol." It is not the same as risk in the sense of "I might lose everything," it is just the typical year-to-year dispersion around the mean.

## The three numbers

For a single asset:

- **Expected return** `μ = E[R]`. The probability-weighted mean of returns. In practice, computed as the historical sample mean, with the silent assumption that the future looks like the sample.
- **Variance** `σ² = E[(R − μ)²]`. The mean squared deviation from the mean. Units are "return squared," which is awkward, so we usually quote:
- **Volatility** `σ = √(σ²)`. The standard deviation, in the same units as returns themselves.

For two assets A and B:

- **Covariance** `Cov(A,B) = E[(R_A − μ_A)(R_B − μ_B)]`. Positive if A and B tend to move together, negative if they move opposite, zero if independent.
- **Correlation** `ρ = Cov(A,B) / (σ_A · σ_B)`. The covariance rescaled to lie in [−1, +1]. Scale-free, so directly comparable across asset pairs.

That is the entire toolkit. Layers 3 through 8 of this curriculum build on these five quantities; nothing else gets added.

## Why correlation matters even more than vol

A portfolio's volatility is NOT the average of its components' volatilities. It is governed by their correlations. Take two assets each with σ = 20%. A 50/50 portfolio of them has:

- σ ≈ 20% if ρ = +1 (perfectly correlated, no benefit from holding both)
- σ ≈ 14.1% if ρ = 0 (independent, vol falls by factor of √2)
- σ ≈ 0% if ρ = −1 (perfect hedge, you have synthesized cash)

That is the math behind diversification. We will work the case in the next lesson; for now, just see that correlation is doing the load-bearing work.

## A subtlety: arithmetic vs geometric mean

When you read "SPY's average return is 10%," check whether that is arithmetic (sum / N) or geometric (the constant rate that would compound to the same end value). Geometric is always lower than arithmetic when there is any volatility, by approximately σ²/2. This is sometimes called the "volatility drag" and it is the reason a portfolio with the same arithmetic mean but lower vol grows faster in the long run.

Example: arithmetic mean 10%, vol 20% → geometric ≈ 10% − (0.20)²/2 = 10% − 2% = 8%. Over 30 years, the difference between compounding at 8% vs 10% is roughly a factor of 1.75. Volatility itself is a cost, not just a discomfort.

## Where the inputs actually come from

You will see textbooks treat μ, σ, ρ as given. In practice all three are estimated from historical data and all three are unstable.

- σ is the most stable: vol of vol is comparatively low, and short-term vol clusters predictably (Layer 5 covers this).
- ρ is unstable but often mean-reverting: stock-bond correlation has flipped sign multiple times since 1970.
- μ is the least stable: equity expected returns may not be estimable to within 3% even with a century of data. This is why everything you build on top of "expected returns" is fragile (Bernstein and Marks both labor on this point).
