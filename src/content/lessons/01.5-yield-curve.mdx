## What the picture means

The **yield curve** plots Treasury yields on the Y-axis against maturity on the X-axis. The shapes have names:

- **Normal / upward-sloping**: short rates low, long rates high. Investors demand more yield for tying money up longer.
- **Flat**: short and long rates similar. Market is unsure whether to lean toward expansion or contraction.
- **Inverted**: short rates HIGHER than long rates. Unusual. Historically a strong (though not infallible) recession signal.
- **Humped**: medium rates above short and long. Rare.

The most-watched single number is the **2s10s spread**: the 10-year yield minus the 2-year yield. Positive = normal, zero = flat, negative = inverted.

## Why curve shape matters

The yield curve is the market's joint forecast of:

1. The path of future short-term rates (= expected future Fed policy).
2. The term premium (extra yield demanded for bearing the interest-rate risk of locking up money for longer).

If the curve inverts, the market is saying: "I expect rates to be LOWER in the future than they are now." The most common reason to expect lower rates is an expected recession (in which the Fed will cut).

The 2s10s has inverted before every US recession since 1978 — usually 6–18 months in advance — with no clean false positives. That track record is part of why bond traders treat it as the most important real-time macro indicator.

## What it does NOT tell you

The curve gives you direction and rough timing on a recession risk. It does not tell you:

- Magnitude. A mild inversion has preceded mild recessions and severe ones alike.
- Equity market timing. Stocks often continue rising for months after an inversion before the recession arrives.
- Which industries get hit. That depends on the recession's character, which the curve doesn't disclose.

The 2022–2024 inversion is a recent stress test of the indicator. It inverted in mid-2022, stayed inverted for record-long durations, and as of this writing the question of "did it forecast a recession that was avoided or one that's still coming" is still being argued. The indicator is not gospel; it's a strong prior.

## Where to track it

Daily yields and the curve are at the [US Treasury daily yield curve page](https://home.treasury.gov/policy-issues/financing-the-government/interest-rate-statistics) and on FRED (DGS2, DGS10, T10Y2Y). Memorize where these live; you'll come back weekly.
