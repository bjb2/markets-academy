## The basic DCF identity

The value of any cash-producing asset is the present value of its future cash flows:

`PV = CF_1 / (1+r)^1 + CF_2 / (1+r)^2 + ... + CF_n / (1+r)^n`

Where:
- CF_t: cash flow in period t
- r: discount rate (typically the asset's cost of capital, derived from CAPM or WACC)
- n: number of periods you forecast explicitly

For a stock, this is conceptually `Value = PV of all future dividends + PV of all future buybacks + terminal value`. In practice, analysts use "free cash flow to equity" (or FCFE) as the cash flow stream that, in steady state, equals dividends + buybacks combined.

## Why three pieces almost always matter

A standard equity DCF has three components:

1. **Explicit forecast period** (typically 5-10 years): year-by-year cash flow estimates based on revenue growth, margin assumptions, capex, working capital.
2. **Terminal value**: the value of all cash flows AFTER the explicit forecast. Usually computed as `TV = CF_terminal × (1+g) / (r - g)` where g is the assumed long-run growth rate. This is the **Gordon Growth Model** applied to the post-explicit-forecast period.
3. **Discount rate** (the WACC for a firm-level DCF, or cost of equity for an equity DCF).

Terminal value is typically 50-80% of total DCF value for a mature company, more for growth companies. Small changes to the terminal growth rate (g) or discount rate (r) move terminal value substantially.

## The terminal value problem

The Gordon Growth Model for terminal value, `TV = CF × (1+g) / (r - g)`, has a famously fragile denominator. As g approaches r, TV explodes.

For a typical large-cap with r = 8-10% and long-run g = 2-3% (about nominal GDP growth), TV is 14-25x the terminal cash flow. Get the discount rate wrong by a percent in either direction, and the multiple changes by 20-30%.

Two pragmatic adjustments:

1. **Sanity-check terminal value against multiples**. If your DCF says TV is 25x the terminal cash flow, but mature companies in this industry trade at 12-15x current FCF, the DCF is implying valuation multiples that haven't been observed. Either your assumptions are too optimistic or the market is wrong. Usually the former.

2. **Use a longer explicit forecast for high-growth companies**. If a company grows at 25%/yr now, projecting only 5 years of explicit forecast pushes a lot of growth into the terminal value (where g is required to drop to 2-3%). Instead, fade growth explicitly over 10-15 years.

## Where DCF works and where it doesn't

DCF works best for:

- Stable, predictable cash flow businesses (mature consumer staples, utilities, integrated oil majors).
- Real estate (rents and growth are observable and contracted).
- Bonds (cash flows are KNOWN; only the discount rate is uncertain).

DCF struggles with:

- High-growth tech (cash flows in the explicit period are tiny; terminal value dominates).
- Pre-revenue or pre-profit companies (no cash flows to project).
- Cyclical businesses (when is "normal"? what discount rate captures cyclicality?).
- Companies with optionality (drug pipelines, R&D-heavy firms; DCF doesn't price the optionality).

In all these cases, multiples (next lesson) or scenario-based valuations are more honest about the uncertainty.

## DCF as a way of thinking, not a precise tool

A common practitioner attitude (Marks, Buffett, McKinsey's <em>Valuation</em> textbook): a DCF's specific number is approximate, but the EXERCISE of building one forces you to confront the actual assumptions. You have to take a view on:

- Revenue growth and how it fades.
- Operating margin (will it expand, contract, hold?).
- Capex intensity and working capital needs.
- Discount rate (cost of equity + cost of debt, weighted).
- Terminal growth rate.

Each assumption is a falsifiable claim about the future. If you build a DCF saying a company is worth 3x its current price, you can ASK: which assumption am I most uncertain about, and what does the world look like if it's wrong? This is more useful than the point estimate.

A useful sensitivity table: vary discount rate and terminal growth rate in a 2D grid; the resulting price range tells you how much your valuation depends on these inputs. If the range is $50-$500 and you're uncertain about the inputs, you've learned that the company's value is just "uncertain."
