## The horizon paradox

A famous empirical observation: equity returns exhibit DIFFERENT statistical patterns at different horizons.

| Horizon | Dominant pattern | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| 1-5 days | Mean reversion | Order-flow imbalance, liquidity, microstructure |
| 1-3 weeks | Mixed | Mean-reverting in indices, somewhat trending in single names |
| 1-12 months | Momentum | Behavioral underreaction, slow information diffusion |
| 3-10 years | Mean reversion | Valuation cycles, business-cycle reversion |

Each horizon has tradable patterns; the patterns are different. A strategy that works at one horizon may FAIL at another. Combining horizons (short-term mean reversion + medium-term momentum) is one way to capture multiple premia, though correlations between strategies need to be analyzed (lesson 3.8).

## Short-horizon mean reversion

Empirical evidence: stocks that have fallen sharply over 1-5 days TEND to bounce in the next 1-5 days. Stocks that have risen sharply tend to give back some.

Examples of validated short-horizon mean reversion signals:

- **RSI(2)**: 2-day Relative Strength Index. When RSI(2) drops below 5 (extremely oversold), buy; when above 95, sell. Larry Connors documented this in detail (multi-decade backtests on SPY). Sharpe in modern markets: 0.5-1.0 on systematic implementation.

- **Bollinger Band reversion**: when price crosses below the lower Bollinger Band (2 standard deviations below a 20-day moving average), buy with an expectation of reversion within 5-10 days.

- **VWAP reversion**: intraday strategy. After an asymmetric move pushes price more than X% away from VWAP, expect partial reversion within hours.

The mechanism: short-term moves are often driven by ORDER-FLOW IMBALANCES (a large institutional sell, a margin-call cascade, a momentum-trader squeeze) rather than fundamental information. When the imbalance is exhausted, the price drifts back toward the pre-move equilibrium.

Limit: small returns per trade (often 0.5-2%). High turnover. Requires execution discipline; transaction costs and slippage eat much of the gross profit.

## Long-horizon mean reversion (valuation cycles)

Over 3-10 year horizons, equity-market and individual-stock valuations cycle around long-term averages. Shiller's CAPE (lesson 2.4) demonstrates this pattern at the index level.

Practical implication: when CAPE is well above its 50-year average (38 in 2021, ~35 in late 2024), forward 10-year real returns tend to be below average. When CAPE is well below its average (sub-15 historically: 2009 reached 13.3), forward 10-year real returns tend to be well above average.

This isn't market timing in the day-trader sense. It's MULTI-YEAR positioning around durable valuation cycles. Combining this with the Marks cycle framework (lesson 6.4) gives an integrated valuation-and-positioning discipline.

For our work, this matters at the broad-portfolio level (when CAPE is elevated, expect lower forward returns; size aggressive strategies accordingly) more than at the individual-trade level.

## Pairs trading and statistical arbitrage

A specialized class of mean-reversion strategies: identify pairs of stocks that historically move together, and trade the SPREAD when it deviates from its historical range.

Example: Coca-Cola and Pepsi. They both face the same macro winds, same retail channels, similar customer bases. Their spread (KO price minus PEP price) has a stable range. When KO outperforms PEP by 5%+ in a month with no obvious reason, the spread is statistically wide. Trade: short KO, long PEP, expecting the spread to revert.

Modern implementations are pair-by-pair, but the framework generalizes to "statistical arbitrage" across hundreds of stocks simultaneously. Long Term Capital Management was a famous (and famous-failed) implementation; modern hedge funds running stat arb (Citadel, Renaissance) achieve high Sharpe with strict risk management.

Retail can implement simple pair trading manually, though the edge has compressed substantially. The "structural reason" pairs trade: cross-sectional behavioral patterns clearing within hours-to-days create the opportunity.

## Where mean reversion fits with momentum

Mean reversion and momentum are NOT competing theories; they operate at different horizons:

- **Short-horizon mean reversion** (1-5 days): captures order-flow inefficiencies
- **Medium-horizon momentum** (1-12 months): captures behavioral underreaction
- **Long-horizon mean reversion** (3-10 years): captures valuation cycles

A diversified active portfolio can run all three simultaneously. Daily-PnL correlations between them are typically moderate (0.2-0.5), so combining produces real diversification (per lesson 3.8).

Our paper-trading-swing-sleeves backtest project is exactly this: adding pullback and RSI(2) sleeves (short-horizon mean reversion) to the existing longcall sleeve (medium-horizon momentum-following via breakout entries). The expectation is that the sleeves are correlated below 0.7 (passing the gate) and produce combined Sharpe better than either alone.

## Cautions

Three failure modes specific to mean-reversion strategies:

### 1. Trend traps

A "broken" stock (deteriorating fundamentals) keeps falling. Mean-reversion signals fire repeatedly; each entry stops out. The strategy bleeds. Defense: filter mean-reversion entries by fundamental quality (don't try to mean-revert on stocks below their 200-day moving average; the longer trend is down for a reason).

### 2. Drawdown clustering

Mean-reversion strategies tend to have CLUSTER LOSSES: when the market is in a trending regime (2010-2014, 2020-2021), mean-reversion strategies underperform. The losses come together. Diversification across strategy types is the structural defense.

### 3. Friction asymmetry

Short-horizon mean-reversion strategies are particularly sensitive to transaction costs. A 1-2% expected gain per trade can be entirely eaten by 30-50 bp of frictions if trade frequency is high. Implementation discipline (limit orders, careful sizing) matters more than for longer-horizon strategies.
