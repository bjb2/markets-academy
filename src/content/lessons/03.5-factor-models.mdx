## The Fama-French breakthrough

<SourceLink id="fama-french-1992" /> is the most-cited paper in empirical finance after CAPM itself. The setup: take all NYSE/AMEX/NASDAQ stocks 1963-1990. Sort them into portfolios by characteristics (size, book-to-market, β, etc.). Measure realized returns. See which characteristics line up with which returns.

The headline result: SIZE and BOOK-TO-MARKET dominate β as predictors of cross-sectional returns. β by itself is nearly useless once you control for size and value. This isn't subtle: when Fama and French sorted only on β, the relationship to returns was essentially flat.

Their proposed model:

`E[R_i] − R_f = β_mkt · MKT + β_smb · SMB + β_hml · HML`

Where:
- **MKT**: market return minus risk-free rate (the CAPM factor)
- **SMB** (Small Minus Big): return on small-cap portfolio minus return on large-cap portfolio
- **HML** (High Minus Low): return on high book-to-market (value) minus low book-to-market (growth)

Each β is the regression sensitivity of the stock to that factor.

## What "factor" actually means

A factor is a long/short portfolio that captures a specific exposure. SMB is constructed by going long a basket of small-cap stocks and short a basket of large-cap stocks, in equal dollar amounts. This long/short construction zeros out market exposure on net, leaving (in theory) pure "size" exposure.

The factor's HISTORICAL RETURN is the factor premium. SMB has historically returned a few percent per year; HML has returned a few percent per year; MKT has returned the equity risk premium. A stock's loading on each factor (its β values) determines its expected return as a linear combination.

A small-cap value stock loads positively on all three factors and gets all three premia. A large-cap growth stock loads positively on MKT but negatively on SMB and HML, so it expects the market premium minus the size and value premia.

## The factor zoo

After Fama-French 1992, the floodgates opened. Academic and practitioner research has documented hundreds of candidate factors. The replicated, durable subset (per AQR, Asness, etc.) is roughly:

- **Market (MKT)**: equity risk premium
- **Size (SMB)**: small > large
- **Value (HML)**: high B/M (or low P/E, low EV/EBITDA) > low B/M
- **Momentum (UMD)**: stocks up over past 12 months (ex last month) > stocks down. NOT in Fama-French 1992; added by Carhart 1997.
- **Quality (QMJ)**: high-profitability stable-growth > low. Fama-French 5-factor (2015) calls this RMW.
- **Low Volatility (BAB)**: low-β > high-β, contradicting CAPM directly. "Betting Against Beta."
- **Investment (CMA)**: companies investing conservatively > aggressively. Fama-French 5-factor.

Each of these has decades of out-of-sample evidence in multiple geographies. Each also has worse Sharpe than its in-sample backtest would suggest (publication bias is real).

Beyond these ~6-7 factors, the "factor zoo" gets dense. Harvey, Liu, Zhu (2016) catalog 300+ published factors and argue most are spurious. The Replication Crisis applies to finance too.

## Are factor premia "compensation for risk" or "behavioral mispricings"?

This is the live academic debate.

**Risk-based view (Fama, Cochrane)**: factor premia exist because the factors carry systematic risk that investors won't bear without compensation. Small caps do badly in recessions; value stocks are distressed in slow-growth regimes; etc. The premia are rational equilibrium pricing.

**Behavioral view (Lakonishok, Shleifer, Vishny; later Asness)**: factor premia exist because investors systematically overpay for glamorous high-growth, large, low-vol stocks and underpay for the unglamorous opposite. Premia are persistent BEHAVIORAL biases, and they should attenuate (but rarely fully disappear) as more capital arbitrages them.

The empirical record:
- Premia have persisted decades after publication: bullish for risk-based view OR for limits-to-arbitrage version of behavioral view.
- Some premia have weakened materially in recent decades (size has been thin since the 1980s; value had a brutal 2010-2020).
- New premia ("low-vol", "quality") have been discovered AFTER the early literature, suggesting researchers keep finding mispricings.

In practice, both views inform real portfolio construction. AQR-style "smart beta" tilts toward documented factors. Berkshire-style concentrated value is the same trade with judgment instead of regression.
