## Inflation has regimes, not levels

It's tempting to think of inflation as a number that bounces around a constant target. The historical record shows something different: inflation operates in *regimes* that can last decades, separated by transition periods of a few years.

Roughly, the last 100 years of US inflation:

- **1933–1965**: low and stable, mostly 0–3%, with occasional war-driven spikes.
- **1965–1982**: structurally high. Multiple peaks above 10%. The era of the "Great Inflation."
- **1982–2020**: disinflationary regime. Volcker broke inflation expectations; subsequent decades trended downward. Average ~2.5%.
- **2020–?**: a regime transition is the open question. The 2021–2023 spike was the largest sustained move since 1982. As of 2026 the question is whether we have returned to the 1982–2020 regime or moved to something structurally higher.

Read Lyn Alden's regime-cycle work at <SourceLink id="lyn-alden-newsletter" /> for one credible framing.

## What changes between regimes

Almost everything investing-relevant:

- **Real returns on cash**: deeply negative in inflationary regimes; positive in disinflationary ones.
- **Bond returns**: a 40-year bull market in bonds (1982–2022) is the disinflation regime in price form. The shift can produce a multi-year bear market.
- **Equity multiples**: contract in inflationary regimes (discount rates higher, real earnings under pressure). Expand in disinflationary regimes. Most P/E "history" is regime-mixed and misleading.
- **Correlations**: stock-bond correlation flips. In disinflation, bonds hedge stocks (correlation ≈ −0.3). In inflation, both fall together (correlation ≈ +0.3 to +0.5). 60/40 portfolios that worked for 40 years stop working.

This is why "inflation regime" is the single highest-leverage macro question for portfolio construction. Get it right and most other decisions cascade.

## How to tell which regime you're in

Honest answer: only with hindsight. In real time you watch:

- **Inflation expectations** (TIPS breakevens, surveys like Michigan and NY Fed).
- **Wage growth** — wages following inflation up is the lock-in mechanism. Once wage expectations shift, inflation gets sticky.
- **Commodity supercycles** — energy and metals tend to lead.
- **Fiscal policy** — sustained large deficits change the long-run inflation picture.
- **Central bank credibility** — the more the Fed is willing to cause unemployment to break inflation expectations, the faster the regime resolves.

The hardest part: regime-change calls are almost always early or wrong before they're right. In 2022 the "we're back to the 70s" call was clearly correct in the moment; in 2024 the "no, the regime held" call looked correct. Both can be right at different time slices.
