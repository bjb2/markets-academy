## One AAPL contract, March 2024

On January 19, 2024, with Apple (AAPL) trading at $182, a call option giving you the right to buy 100 shares at $185 by March 15, 2024 cost roughly $4.50 per share, or $450 for the contract. If AAPL closed above $185 on expiry, you could buy at $185 and immediately hold stock worth more. If it closed at or below $185, the option expired worthless and you lost $450, full stop. A shareholder who bought 100 shares that day at $182 had $18,200 at risk. The option buyer had $450 at risk.

That tradeoff is the whole game in one example.

## The vocabulary

Every option has five attributes:

- **Underlying:** the asset the option is written on (AAPL stock, SPY, crude oil futures).
- **Strike (K):** the price at which you may buy (call) or sell (put).
- **Expiry (T):** the date the right lapses. After this date, the option is gone.
- **Premium:** what you pay today for the right. It is paid upfront and is not refundable.
- **Type:** a **call** gives the right to buy; a **put** gives the right to sell.

**Moneyness** describes where the stock price (S) sits relative to the strike (K) right now:

| Term | Condition | Meaning |
|------|-----------|---------|
| In-the-money (ITM) | Call: S > K, Put: S < K | Would have positive value if exercised today |
| At-the-money (ATM) | S ≈ K | Strike equals current price |
| Out-of-the-money (OTM) | Call: S < K, Put: S > K | Would be worthless if exercised today |

The $185 call in the AAPL example was OTM when purchased: the stock was at $182 and the strike was $185. The buyer needed a $3+ move just to reach the money.

A **put** is the mirror. A put gives the right to sell at the strike. You buy a put when you expect the stock to fall, or to protect a position you already own. A put on AAPL with a $175 strike would gain value if AAPL dropped below $175 before expiry.

## Why this matters going forward

Options are not primarily speculation tools. They are **risk-transfer instruments**: buyers transfer downside risk to sellers in exchange for a premium. Every position in the longcall sleeve is long calls, meaning you know precisely what you can lose before you enter.

Three things follow immediately from understanding the payoff at expiry:

**Break-even is strike plus premium.** A call is profitable only if the stock rises past strike + premium. Buying ATM options and expecting "any move up" to profit is a common beginner error.

**Leverage is asymmetric.** In case (d) above, the stock moved 15% and the option returned 4.5x. But in case (a), the stock moved -4% and the option returned -100%. The leverage amplifies both directions, with a hard floor at zero.

**Puts are insurance.** A put on a stock you own limits your downside to strike - premium paid.
