## Three commodity-like asset classes

| Class | Examples | Supply driver | Demand driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Commodities** | Crude oil, gold, copper, wheat, corn | Production cost, geopolitics, weather | Industrial use, store-of-value (gold), strategic stockpiles |
| **FX** | USD, EUR, JPY, GBP | Central bank policy, money supply | Trade flows, capital flows, store-of-value |
| **Crypto** | BTC, ETH, stablecoins | Algorithmic issuance, mining costs | Speculation, transactional use, store-of-value narrative |

None of these throw off cash flows in the way equities do. They are priced by supply-demand interaction with the relevant use case anchoring the LEVEL.

## Commodities

The classical theory (Hotelling 1931 for exhaustible resources; supply-demand for renewable):

- **Industrial commodities** (oil, copper, iron ore, lithium): price moves with global industrial activity. Strong inverse correlation with USD (commodities priced in USD; weaker dollar mechanically raises non-USD-denominated demand). Long-cycle supply response: oil takes 5-7 years from greenfield discovery to first barrel; new copper mines take 10-15 years.
- **Agricultural commodities** (wheat, corn, soy): annual supply cycle; weather-driven. Government intervention significant (subsidies, tariffs, strategic reserves). Storage and quality issues.
- **Precious metals** (gold, silver, platinum): primarily store-of-value (gold) plus some industrial demand. Gold has the longest store-of-value history (4,000+ years); its monetary role survived the gold standard's end and persists in central bank reserves.

Commodities don't compound the way equities do; you can't "buy and hold" gold for 30 years and expect the same kind of growth as SPY. Gold's REAL return over the very long run has been close to zero. Equities' long-run real return has been around 6-7%. The difference is intrinsic: equities reinvest cash flows; gold sits there.

Investors hold commodities for INFLATION HEDGING (real assets retain purchasing power when fiat depreciates), CRISIS DIVERSIFICATION (gold has a low correlation with stocks in many regimes), and TACTICAL TRADING (cycles, momentum, mean reversion within commodity bull/bear cycles).

## FX (foreign exchange)

The largest financial market by daily volume (~$7.5T/day). Exchanges currency pairs (EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD, etc.). Prices reflect:

- **Interest rate differentials**: higher-rate currency tends to appreciate (carry trade flows in); lower-rate depreciates.
- **Trade flows**: trade surplus countries see structural currency demand; deficit countries see structural supply.
- **Capital flows**: foreign portfolio investment, FDI, central bank reserves, sovereign wealth funds.
- **Inflation differentials**: countries with higher inflation see currency depreciation (purchasing power parity).
- **Political risk premia**: instability, sanctions, default risk depress the currency.

For US retail, FX is mostly an indirect exposure: international stocks and bonds expose you to FX implicitly. Direct FX speculation is a small niche; most retail FX trading is high-cost and produces poor outcomes (high leverage, wide spreads, tax inefficiency).

A useful institutional concept: **carry trade**. Borrow in a low-yielding currency (JPY, historically), invest in a high-yielding currency (AUD, BRL, ZAR). Earn the rate differential as long as the high-yielder doesn't depreciate by more than the differential. Works in calm regimes; explodes catastrophically in risk-off episodes (the 2008 JPY carry unwind was a global financial event).

## Crypto

A separate animal that's hard to classify cleanly:

- **Bitcoin** (BTC): closest to "digital gold" framing. Fixed supply (21M coins). Volatile (50-80% annual vol historically). Store-of-value thesis from the bitcoin community. Highly correlated with risk-on/risk-off sentiment despite the "uncorrelated alternative" pitch.
- **Ethereum** (ETH): platform layer with programmable contracts (DeFi, NFTs, etc.). Fundamental "yield" exists via staking. Closer to equity-like cash flows than Bitcoin.
- **Stablecoins** (USDT, USDC): pegged to USD; function as on-chain cash. Massive transactional volume; mostly used for moving value between exchanges and DeFi protocols.
- **Altcoins**: thousands of others, mostly speculation-driven.

The honest summary of crypto's investment case as of writing (mid-2026):

- BTC has produced extraordinary long-run returns from 2010-2020. Returns since 2021 have been more modest.
- BTC's correlation with risk assets (NASDAQ in particular) has been higher than the "digital gold" narrative suggests.
- ETH and other platform tokens have an actual underlying use case but valuation methods are unsettled.
- The asset class is high-vol, regulation-sensitive, and exposed to operational risks (exchange failures, key management, smart contract bugs) absent from traditional asset classes.

A reasonable retail position size for crypto, if held at all, is small (1-5% of portfolio for typical investors; some crypto-focused investors hold much more, accepting the vol). Treating it as an asset class on par with equities or fixed income overstates its current institutional standing.

## Speculation vs investment

A useful distinction (Graham, Bogle, Marks all use it):

- **Investment**: ownership of an asset that produces cash flows over time. Value can be derived from those cash flows. Return comes from (a) cash flows received and (b) sale price.
- **Speculation**: bet on the price of an asset whose value is anchored by expected sale price to another buyer. Return depends entirely on the next buyer paying more.

Stocks: mostly investment (cash flows exist, even if you don't receive them as dividends, the company reinvests them).
Bonds: investment (cash flows are contractual).
Real estate: investment (rents) or speculation (flipping).
Commodities: typically speculation (no cash flows; bet on the next buyer).
FX: speculation (no cash flows; carry trades earn interest differential but the price level is speculation).
Crypto: speculation (BTC), or partially investment (ETH with staking, DeFi tokens with revenue).
Art, collectibles: pure speculation.

Speculation isn't a moral category, it's a structural one. Speculation can be profitable. But it's a fundamentally different game: there's no anchor to value, only an evolving consensus about what others will pay. The Keynesian "beauty contest" framing is exact: you're trying to predict what others will predict that others will think.

The investment-vs-speculation frame matters because the SKILL involved is different. Investment rewards careful fundamental analysis. Speculation rewards reading sentiment, momentum, and crowd dynamics. Conflating the two leads to applying the wrong tools.
