## What the order book contains

A central limit order book (CLOB) is two sorted lists:

- **Bids**: resting BUY orders, sorted highest price first. The top is the **best bid**.
- **Asks** (or **offers**): resting SELL orders, sorted lowest price first. The top is the **best ask**.

The difference between best bid and best ask is the **spread**. The midpoint is sometimes called the **mid**. The top of book (best bid + best ask) is what gets disseminated to retail price feeds.

Each level has a price and a SIZE (number of shares or contracts available at that price). Real books have orders aggregated by price at the displayed level, with priority by time of arrival within each price (price-time priority).

## Two order types

| Order type | Behavior | Cost / risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Market** | Take liquidity immediately. Walks the opposite side of the book from inside out. | Pays the spread + market impact. Guaranteed fill, uncertain price. |
| **Limit** | Adds liquidity at a specified price. Joins the book and waits. | Pays no spread (or earns it as maker rebate). Risks no fill if market moves away. |

Many secondary types (stop, stop-limit, iceberg, peg) are combinations and variations. The two primitives above generate them all.

A useful frame: market orders DEMAND immediacy and pay for it. Limit orders SUPPLY immediacy and get paid for it (sometimes literally, via maker rebates).

## What happens when an order arrives

The matching engine's logic is roughly:

```
On incoming order O:
  if O is a market order:
    while O has remaining quantity:
      take the best opposite-side level
      fill min(O.remaining, level.size) at level.price
      if level.size > O.remaining: reduce level.size; done
      else: remove level entirely
  elif O is a limit order:
    if O can cross (buy limit >= best ask, or sell limit <= best bid):
      fill the crossable portion like a market order
    if O has remaining quantity:
      add it to the book at O.price, time-priority at that level
```

That is the entire core of every electronic exchange in equities, futures, options, and crypto. The variations are in WHO can put orders in (members, retail via brokers, market makers), in WHAT incentives are offered for resting orders (maker rebates, payment for order flow), and in WHERE orders are routed (lit exchanges, dark pools, internalizers; Layer 4.5 covers that).

## What you see vs what's there

Retail price feeds typically show only the best bid and ask (top of book). Some brokers (Interactive Brokers, Tradestation) show the full depth ladder. Exchange members and direct-feed subscribers see the full book.

What you DON'T see, even in full-depth feeds:

- **Hidden / iceberg orders**: orders with a displayed size smaller than their total size. Common from institutions hiding the true magnitude of their flow.
- **Dark pool resting orders**: parallel venues where orders are matched without public quotes (Layer 4.5).
- **Off-exchange internalized fills**: retail orders are often filled at the broker's house against other retail or against the broker's own inventory, never touching a public book.

So the visible book is a partial view of available liquidity. For retail-sized orders this is rarely material; for institutional-sized orders it dominates execution strategy.

## Why the book exists

Auctions are the oldest matching mechanism (open-outcry pit). Continuous limit-order books beat auctions for high-frequency trading because:

1. Time priority creates a credible incentive to post liquidity early.
2. Best price is always discoverable: read top of book.
3. Asynchronous matching scales: thousands of trades per second, no central auctioneer.

Modern equity exchanges run continuous CLOBs throughout the day, bookended by opening and closing AUCTIONS (lesson 4.7) that aggregate orders into a single clearing price at the bell. The hybrid combines each mechanism's strengths.
