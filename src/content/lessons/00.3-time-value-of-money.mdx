## A dollar today vs a dollar in a year

A dollar in your hand today is worth more than a dollar promised next year — even if there is no inflation and you fully trust the counterparty. Why? Because the dollar today can be invested, consumed, or used to remove optionality (pay down debt, settle a worry). The promised dollar has none of those properties yet.

The exchange rate between today-dollars and future-dollars is called the **discount rate**. Most discount rates have three layered components:

1. A risk-free real rate (compensation for delay)
2. Expected inflation (so you're paid back in equivalent purchasing power)
3. A risk premium (compensation for the chance the promise breaks)

Add them and you get a nominal discount rate — what the market actually quotes.

## The two equations you actually need

**Present value of a single future cash flow:**

```
PV = FV / (1 + r)^n
```

Where `FV` is the future value, `r` is the per-period discount rate, and `n` is the number of periods.

**Future value of a present amount:**

```
FV = PV × (1 + r)^n
```

Same equation, solved the other direction. Everything else in fixed-income math is variations on these two.

## A concrete example

You're promised $100 in 10 years. The market discount rate for that credit is 6% annually. What's it worth today?

```
PV = 100 / (1.06)^10 = 100 / 1.7908 ≈ $55.84
```

If the same $100 is promised in 30 years at the same 6%:

```
PV = 100 / (1.06)^30 ≈ $17.41
```

A factor-of-3 difference in time horizon produces a factor-of-3 difference in present value. This sensitivity is why long-duration assets (30-year bonds, growth stocks, real estate) reprice violently when rates move.

## The discount rate is the most important number in finance

Almost every valuation question reduces to "what's the right discount rate?" Get the cash flows roughly right and the discount rate roughly right, and you've done 90% of the analysis. Most disagreements between bulls and bears on a stock are not disagreements about cash flows — they're disagreements about discount rates.

Carry this into every later lesson: when someone tells you a stock is "worth" X, the next question is *at what discount rate*.
