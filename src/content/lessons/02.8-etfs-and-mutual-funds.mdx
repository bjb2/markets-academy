## Two structures, near-identical economics

A **mutual fund** is a pooled investment that:
- Issues and redeems shares ONCE per day at NAV (net asset value), computed after market close.
- Sends redemption cash within 1-2 days.
- Charges an expense ratio plus sometimes loads (front-end or back-end sales charges).

An **ETF (exchange-traded fund)** is a pooled investment that:
- Trades continuously on an exchange like a stock.
- Creates and redeems large blocks (typically 50,000 shares) through Authorized Participants (APs) who arbitrage any discrepancy between the ETF's market price and the NAV of its holdings.
- Charges an expense ratio; no loads.

Both pool capital into a basket of underlying holdings; both pay you a proportional share of dividends and interest; both report holdings periodically. The main differences:

| Dimension | Mutual fund | ETF |
|---|---|---|
| Trading | Once per day at NAV | Continuous; market price ± small premium/discount |
| Tax efficiency | Triggers capital gain distributions for ALL holders when fund sells appreciated positions | In-kind creation/redemption avoids most capital gain triggers; very tax-efficient |
| Fees | 0.03% to 1%+ range | 0.03% to 0.75% range |
| Minimum | Sometimes $1,000-$3,000+ | One share (often $50-$500) |
| Intraday flexibility | None | Full (limit orders, stops, options on it) |

The TAX EFFICIENCY of ETFs is genuine and meaningful. When a mutual fund holder redeems, the fund may have to sell underlying holdings, triggering capital gains that are distributed to ALL remaining holders. ETF redemptions go through APs via in-kind exchanges (AP delivers ETF shares, receives basket of underlying), so no taxable sale happens inside the fund. Over decades, this can mean 0.5-1% per year less drag from capital gains taxes for taxable accounts holding ETFs vs equivalent mutual funds.

## The Authorized Participant mechanism

ETF prices stay tight to NAV because of arbitrage:

If ETF market price > NAV (ETF trading rich):
- AP buys the underlying basket on exchanges.
- AP delivers the basket to the issuer in exchange for newly-CREATED ETF shares.
- AP sells the new ETF shares at the premium.
- Profit: premium minus a few bp of transaction cost.
- Effect: ETF supply increases, premium compresses toward NAV.

If ETF market price < NAV (ETF trading cheap):
- AP buys ETF shares cheap on exchange.
- Redeems them with the issuer, receiving the underlying basket worth NAV.
- Sells the basket on exchanges.
- Profit: discount minus transaction cost.
- Effect: ETF supply decreases, discount compresses.

The arbitrage works to within a basis point or two for liquid ETFs (SPY, QQQ, IWM); a few basis points for moderately-traded ones; can break down for thinly-traded ETFs in volatile markets (e.g., bond ETFs during March 2020 traded at 4-7% discounts to stale NAV for hours).

## Index funds vs active mutual funds

Mutual funds and ETFs come in two flavors:

- **Index (passive) funds**: track a specific benchmark (S&P 500, Russell 2000, MSCI EAFE). Holdings are mechanical; expense ratios are very low (0.03-0.10% common). Performance matches benchmark minus fees.
- **Active funds**: manager picks securities trying to beat a benchmark. Expense ratios higher (0.4-1.2% typical; some funds charge 1.5%+). Performance varies widely; mostly trails the benchmark net of fees over long horizons.

The next lesson (2.9) covers the empirical case for passive in depth. For now: passive index funds and ETFs are the dominant DEFAULT for retail and a growing default for institutional. Total US ETF assets crossed $10T in 2024; passive mutual funds have surpassed active in equity assets.

## What's INSIDE the fund: the holdings file

Both mutual funds and ETFs publish their holdings:
- Mutual funds: quarterly (sometimes monthly).
- ETFs: daily (mostly; some specialized ETFs are quarterly).

Pull the holdings file for any ETF you're considering. For SPY you'll find ~500 stocks with cap-weighted percentages. For a sector ETF you'll find that sector's universe with the index methodology's weights. For a thematic ETF you'll find the manager's interpretation of the theme, which is often more arbitrary than the marketing suggests.

This is where "smart beta" lives: ETFs that tilt the holdings toward documented factor exposures (value, momentum, quality, low-vol). They're still systematic (the rule is public) but they deviate from cap-weighted benchmarks. Expense ratios 0.15-0.45%.

## Mutual fund classes (briefly)

Mutual funds historically came in multiple share classes with different fee structures:

- **A-shares**: front-end load (e.g., 5% on purchase), lower ongoing fee.
- **B-shares**: back-end load that fades over years, higher ongoing fee. Mostly retired.
- **C-shares**: no load but higher annual fee, often 12b-1 distribution fees.
- **Institutional / I-shares**: lowest fees, minimums of $100K-$1M+.
- **No-load funds (Investor / Admiral)**: no front- or back-end load; pricing primarily through expense ratio. Vanguard, Fidelity Zero, Schwab Equity Index lines all follow this model.

Modern retail brokerages (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard) sell no-load funds without commissions. Loads are largely an artifact of older broker-sold distribution channels.

## ETF gotchas worth knowing

- **Tracking error**: an ETF's NAV may drift slightly from the benchmark due to sampling, replication, or fees. Trivial for liquid US-equity ETFs; noticeable for international, factor, leveraged.
- **Leveraged / inverse ETFs (SQQQ, TQQQ, UVXY)**: REBALANCE DAILY to target leverage. Over multi-day periods, their performance diverges from "Nx the index" in path-dependent ways. They are short-term tactical instruments, not long-term holdings.
- **Tax structure variations**: most equity ETFs are RIC (regulated investment companies). Some commodity ETFs (USO, GLD) are partnerships or grantor trusts, taxed differently. Read the prospectus.
- **Bid-ask spread**: liquid ETFs (SPY, QQQ) have 1¢ spreads. Less-liquid ETFs can have 10-50 bp spreads, eating into the value of holding the structure. Check median spread before buying.
