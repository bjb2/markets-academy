## The distinction Knight drew in 1921

Frank Knight separated two concepts that are usually conflated:

- **Risk**: situations where you can list the possible outcomes and assign probabilities to each. Card games. Insured fires. The roulette wheel.
- **Uncertainty**: situations where you cannot meaningfully assign probabilities, either because the outcome space isn't enumerable, or because past frequencies are not a guide to future frequencies. Wars. Technological transitions. The introduction of a new currency.

Markets contain both. The question of "will this stock be up or down tomorrow" is risk-shaped (you can model volatility and assign a distribution). The question of "will this technology displace the entire incumbent industry within 5 years" is uncertainty-shaped.

## Why the distinction matters

If you confuse the two, you do dangerous things:

- You apply Gaussian statistics to fat-tailed processes. (Most option pricing models did this in 2007. It cost trillions.)
- You assume past returns "give you" the future return distribution. Past returns describe the past regime. Regimes change.
- You overweight precise-looking forecasts because they have decimal points. (A 14.3% forecast feels more credible than "between 8 and 25%." It is not.)

Taleb's career, read <SourceLink id="taleb-essays" /> for the long form, is built on the observation that most catastrophic losses come from uncertainty mistakenly handled as risk. The trader who blows up in a 6-sigma event is the trader who modeled the world as Gaussian when the underlying process was Pareto-tailed.

## Knightian uncertainty in your own decisions

When you're considering a trade, position, or career move, ask: *is this risk or uncertainty?*

If risk: you can size by Kelly, hedge, model. Standard tools apply.

If uncertainty: standard tools mislead. The honest response is to (1) keep the position small relative to ruin, (2) buy optionality where available, (3) prefer reversible bets over irreversible ones, and (4) survive long enough to learn.

The longcall sleeve we run takes a Knightian view of upside (where will the AI infra capex cycle go? Unknowable) and a risk view of downside (premium can decay to zero, bound by structure). That asymmetry is the whole point.
