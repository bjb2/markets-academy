## How a Fed decision actually transmits

The FOMC announces a 25 bp hike at 2:00pm ET. What happens in the next hour, day, and month?

**Within seconds:**
- The fed funds futures market reprices the path of future rate decisions.
- The 2-year Treasury yield jumps the most (it's most sensitive to near-term Fed expectations).
- The 10-year yield moves less (it's a weighted average of expected future short rates over a longer horizon plus a term premium).
- Equity indices reprice based on (a) higher discount rates for future cash flows and (b) implicit signal about the Fed's read on the economy.
- The dollar usually strengthens on a hike (higher rates attract foreign capital).

**Within weeks:**
- Banks adjust their prime rate (= fed funds + 3 percentage points, by convention).
- New mortgage rates reset (anchored to the 10-year).
- Corporate paper and credit-card rates reset.
- High-yield issuance slows if rates moved hawkishly.

**Within months:**
- Mortgage applications decline (housing is highly rate-sensitive).
- Auto loans slow.
- Capex decisions get re-examined at the new hurdle rate.
- Hiring decelerates.

**Within quarters to years:**
- Inflation responds. The famous "long and variable lags" — Friedman estimated 12–18 months. Modern estimates are similar.

The reason the Fed acts on tiny rate changes in a meaningful way is that the entire credit pricing structure of the economy is calibrated to fed funds. A 25 bp move propagates through trillions of dollars of contracts.

## Open market operations: the mechanics

When the Fed wants to expand reserves, the NY Fed's trading desk (the "open market desk") buys Treasury securities from primary dealers. The dealers' bank accounts at the Fed get credited with new reserves; the Fed receives the securities and adds them to its balance sheet.

Conversely, the Fed can sell securities to drain reserves. Or it can let maturing securities roll off without reinvesting — "quantitative tightening" (QT), the slower-motion version.

Post-2008, OMO scale changed dramatically. The Fed's balance sheet went from ~$900B pre-crisis to ~$4.5T after QE3, peaked at ~$9T in 2022, and as of 2026 is around the $6T range as QT runs.

## What to actually watch

For day-to-day macro reading: SOFR (replacement for LIBOR), fed funds target range, 2-year Treasury yield, 10-year Treasury yield, the 2s10s spread (curve slope). Five numbers. Most macro stories reduce to changes in these five.
