## Three distinct Buffett eras

Reading the Berkshire shareholder letters from 1977 onward (<SourceLink id="buffett-letters" />) reveals three distinct phases of Buffett's investing approach:

### Era 1: Graham purist (1957-1972)

Buffett ran a partnership in Omaha applying Graham's net-net and "cigar butt" approach. Cigar butts: discarded stocks that have "one more puff" of value left even though they're being thrown away. Examples: Dempster Mill Manufacturing, Berkshire Hathaway itself (originally a failing textile mill), Sanborn Map.

The strategy worked but was scale-limited: net-nets are small companies; running large capital in them meant being a meaningful fraction of float. Buffett dissolved the partnership in 1969 in part because he saw the strategy's diminishing applicability at increasing scale.

### Era 2: Quality at fair price (1972-1995)

The See's Candies acquisition in January 1972 is the inflection. Berkshire paid $25M for See's, then earning ~$2M/yr (P/E 12.5x, not cheap by Graham standards). Over the next 50 years See's earned over $2B, a more than 8,000% return on Berkshire's original investment, without significant capital reinvestment. The business produces pricing power year after year; Buffett raised prices regularly, customers absorbed them, returns on invested capital stayed at 25-50%.

The lesson Buffett extracted: a business with durable competitive advantages (a "moat") generates returns that compound for decades. The entry price matters but is dominated by long-term economic value generation.

Subsequent Era 2 investments: Capital Cities/ABC, Coca-Cola (1988, the largest single position for years), American Express, Washington Post Company, GEICO (acquired fully 1996).

### Era 3: Scale challenges and adaptation (1995-present)

Berkshire's size eventually outgrew the universe of public companies where Buffett could deploy meaningful capital. He shifted to:
- Wholly-owned operating businesses (BNSF Railway 2009 was the largest single acquisition, $44B).
- Berkshire Hathaway Energy (utility-like infrastructure).
- Index-fund-like positions in large-caps (Apple position grew to $150B+ at peak, behaving like a leveraged S&P bet).
- Cash (Berkshire's cash and Treasury bills have grown to $150B+, partly because deployment opportunities at Berkshire's scale are limited).

This phase is less about applying value principles and more about managing capital that exceeds what value strategies can absorb. Buffett's letters in this era often acknowledge size as a meaningful drag on returns ("Size is the anchor on performance").

## The Buffett synthesis

Combining the three eras, Buffett's mature framework has five elements:

### 1. Margin of safety (from Graham)

Buy below intrinsic value. The buffer protects against errors. Still required, just with a broader sense of what counts as intrinsic value.

### 2. Quality of the business

Look for:
- Durable competitive advantage (moat).
- High return on invested capital (15-25%+ sustained).
- Pricing power (ability to raise prices without losing customers).
- Owner-friendly management with proven capital allocation.
- Simple, understandable business model.

### 3. Time horizon

"Our favorite holding period is forever." For wonderful businesses, the compound machine works best when undisturbed. Tax efficiency and the absence of trading costs are additional structural benefits.

### 4. Concentration

Buffett held 60%+ of his fund in his top 5 ideas for most of the partnership era. Berkshire still concentrates: Apple was 40%+ of the equity portfolio at peak. Diversification is the substitute for knowledge; concentration is appropriate when you have high conviction and durable theses.

### 5. Circle of competence

Only invest in businesses you can EVALUATE. "What counts for most people in investing is not how much they know, but rather how realistically they define what they don't know." Buffett famously avoided tech for decades (until late 2000s with IBM, then Apple) because he didn't trust his ability to evaluate technological change.

## Capital allocation and the BURDEN of cash

A useful Buffett concept that Layer 2.2 hinted at: the DOLLAR TEST for retained capital. Each dollar a company retains should produce at least one dollar of additional market value. If retention destroys value (ROIC < cost of capital), the company should return the cash.

Buffett judges CEOs by this standard. Many "good operators" are bad capital allocators: they retain earnings and invest in projects that earn less than cost of capital, destroying value over decades while remaining "profitable" on standard metrics. Buffett's preference for capital-light businesses (See's, Apple's hardware-and-services model) reflects this: less capital reinvestment required for growth = more cash returnable to shareholders.

For Berkshire itself, Buffett has historically deployed retained earnings into either (a) new acquisitions, (b) increased positions in existing holdings, or (c) buying back Berkshire stock when it trades below intrinsic value. He has explicitly avoided dividends, arguing that Berkshire's reinvestment IRR has exceeded what shareholders would earn distributing the cash. The dollar test is met.

## Where Buffett's approach has weakened or evolved

Three honest qualifications:

### 1. Tech avoidance was a real cost

Buffett admitted in multiple letters that staying away from tech (Microsoft, Google, Amazon during their highest-return eras) cost Berkshire significant returns. He acknowledged Bezos as one of the greatest CEOs in history but didn't invest because he couldn't predict Amazon's cash-flow trajectory. The circle of competence is necessary; it also has a cost.

### 2. Size has become a structural drag

Berkshire's stock has underperformed the S&P 500 over the last 15-20 years (though still earning solid absolute returns). The reason isn't a failure of philosophy; it's that elephant-sized positions are hard to find. Berkshire's "advantage" is harder to apply when the universe of investable opportunities at scale is small.

### 3. Index funds have absorbed the easy lessons

When Buffett first ran his partnership, broad-market index funds didn't exist. The systematic value premium was harder to capture passively. Today, anyone can buy a value-tilted ETF for 0.05% expense ratio; the easy parts of Buffett's framework are commoditized. The remaining edge requires the harder pieces: business quality assessment, capital allocation judgment, behavioral discipline.

## Practical takeaways

For an active investor, Buffett's framework offers:

1. A focus on BUSINESS QUALITY as a more durable source of returns than statistical cheapness.
2. The dollar test as a useful capital-allocation lens for evaluating management.
3. Time horizon as an investing edge (most investors trade too much; buffeting holding period is a feature, not a bug).
4. Concentration when conviction is high; diversification when it isn't.
5. Circle of competence as a discipline (admit what you don't know).

For our paper-trading-longcall project, the Buffett framework guides the underlying-name selection: prefer high-ROIC businesses with durable moats over lottery-like high-vol names without identifiable moats. The options strategy is the wrapper; the business quality is what makes the trade structurally sound rather than just statistically cheap.
