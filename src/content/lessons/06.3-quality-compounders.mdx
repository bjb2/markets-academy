## The math of compounders

Two businesses, both reinvesting 50% of earnings:

| Business | ROIC | Annual growth in book/share | 30-yr book multiplier |
|---|---|---|---|
| Average S&P | 12% | 6% | 5.7x |
| Quality compounder | 30% | 15% | 66.2x |
| Elite compounder | 40% | 20% | 237x |

The "magic" isn't growth rate alone; it's growth rate sustained at SCALE. Most growth businesses see their growth rate decay rapidly as they get larger (the law of large numbers). True quality compounders are companies that defend their high ROIC and growth for decades.

The mechanism: compounding gets exponentially more valuable as horizons extend. 15% for 5 years is unremarkable; 15% for 30 years is wealth creation.

## What a moat actually is

Pat Dorsey (Morningstar; <em>The Little Book That Builds Wealth</em>) categorizes moats into five types:

### 1. Cost advantage

The producer can deliver goods at structurally lower cost than competitors:
- **Scale economies**: fixed costs spread over more volume (Walmart, Amazon).
- **Process advantage**: proprietary methods (early Geico's direct-distribution insurance model).
- **Resource access**: privileged inputs (mining companies on the lowest-cost deposits, US oil sands operators with the cheapest extraction costs).

A cost advantage is durable as long as the competitor can't replicate the input or scale. Tends to be strongest in commodity-like businesses where price is the dominant competitive variable.

### 2. Network effects

Each new user makes the product more valuable for existing users. Telephones, social networks, payment systems, ridesharing platforms all exhibit this. The dominant player tends to win; second-place competitors face an uphill battle because their network is smaller.

Network effects are extremely durable when present. They also produce winner-take-most outcomes; identifying the winner ex ante is hard.

### 3. Switching costs

Customers face friction (financial, learning curve, integration) to switch to a competitor. Enterprise software (Microsoft Office, Salesforce, SAP), payment processors, banks for primary depository relationships, surgical equipment standards in hospitals.

Switching-cost moats are strong because customers WANT to be locked in (the alternative is constant re-evaluation). The lock-in is mutually beneficial in steady state.

### 4. Intangibles: brand, regulatory, intellectual property

- **Brand**: consumer associations and trust. Coke, Nike, Disney, Apple in their respective categories. Pricing power comes from brand premium.
- **Regulatory**: government-granted advantages. Pharmaceutical patents, broadcasting licenses, casino licenses, certain financial regulations.
- **IP**: patents, proprietary technology, trade secrets. Pharma patents are the cleanest case; many tech moats include IP components.

Intangibles are durable as long as the underlying right is enforceable and consumer preferences/regulations don't change.

### 5. Efficient scale

A specific case where the market is "just big enough" for one or two incumbents and any new entrant would be unprofitable. Pipelines, railroads in non-overlapping geographies, some niche industrial markets. The barrier isn't that new entry is difficult; it's that new entry would destroy profitability for everyone.

## Sustained ROIC as the diagnostic

You can identify potential quality compounders by their financial track record. Look for:

- **ROIC sustained at 20%+ for 10+ years.** This is rare; the durability is itself the moat signal.
- **Growing revenue and earnings through multiple cycles**, not just in expansions.
- **Pricing power**: real-price increases that customers absorb.
- **Capital-light growth**: ability to grow without proportionally heavy capital spending.
- **High free cash flow conversion**: most earnings become cash, not stuck in working capital or assets.

A useful screen for US large-caps: ROIC > 20%, debt/equity < 1, FCF margin > 15%, 10-year revenue growth > 8%. The names that show up are the usual suspects (LVMH, Costco, ASML, Microsoft, Visa, Mastercard, Apple, etc.) plus some less-famous compounders that produced excellent returns over decades.

## Why quality compounders trade at premium multiples

A natural concern: "If quality compounders are so great, won't the market figure that out and price them at infinite multiples?" Partially yes:

- Quality compounders DO trade at higher multiples (P/E 25-40x typical) than the market average (~18x).
- The premium reflects the long-term growth math. At higher growth rates with sustained high ROIC, the DCF math justifies higher multiples.
- BUT: quality eventually fails. Some compounders lose their moat (Kodak, Sears, Macy's, IBM through various eras). The market sometimes prices in PERMANENT quality where actual quality is temporary. This is when overpaying for "compounders" produces disappointing returns.

Buffett's See's Candies math: paying 30x P/E for a 25-30% ROIC business that compounds for 50 years still produces excellent IRRs. Paying 30x for a business that compounds for 5 years and then stalls produces mediocre returns. The DURATION of the moat is the load-bearing assumption.

## The compounder critique: Marks' "There They Go Again" memo

Howard Marks (lesson 6.4) wrote a memo in 2007 warning that the "compounder" / "growth at any price" thesis was being applied to companies that didn't deserve it. He observed that compounder investing has cycles: in expansions, EVERY growth company looks like a compounder; in contractions, only the genuine ones survive.

The Marks framework: distinguish between MULTIPLE EXPANSION (the market re-rating a growth company higher; ephemeral) and FUNDAMENTAL GROWTH (the company actually growing intrinsic value; durable). Most "compounder" returns in late-cycle markets are multiple expansion. When the cycle turns, multiples compress and only the genuine compounders maintain growth.

The discipline: own quality compounders bought during cyclical drawdowns or sustained periods of unloved-ness, not at peak multiples. The 2020 COVID dip provided this in many compounders; the 2022 multi-compression provided it in others. Patience to wait for these moments is the practitioner's edge.

## Where to study compounders

The literature is substantial:
- Pat Dorsey, <em>The Little Book That Builds Wealth</em> (the moat taxonomy).
- Buffett shareholder letters (decades of compounder commentary).
- Terry Smith's Fundsmith annual letters (the modern "quality" school).
- Saber Capital Management quarterly letters (specific Buffett-style portfolio commentary).
- 100 Baggers (Mayer) and 100 to 1 in the Stock Market (Phelps): historical compounder studies.

Each is rich with case studies and frameworks. The reading list for serious students of compounding is substantially longer than the lessons here can cover.
