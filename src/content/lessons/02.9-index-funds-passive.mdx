## Bogle's argument

John Bogle (founder of Vanguard, 1929-2019) formalized the case for passive investing in <em>Common Sense Investing</em> and decades of writing. The core argument:

1. **All investors collectively ARE the market.** Active and passive investors together own every share. The aggregate return = market return.
2. **Passive investors earn the market return MINUS their fees** (typically a few basis points). They by definition track the index.
3. **Active investors EARN the market return MINUS their fees**, BUT their fees are much higher (1%+ in the US historically).
4. **Therefore: passive investors as a group, after fees, outperform active investors as a group, after fees.** This is arithmetic, not empirical.

The empirical part is showing that DISPERSION around the average doesn't help individual investors much because picking winners in advance is hard.

## The S&P SPIVA report

S&P publishes the SPIVA (S&P Indices Versus Active) report annually. Across markets and styles, the headline numbers (2024 SPIVA US):

| Time horizon | % of US large-cap active funds beaten by S&P 500 |
|---|---|
| 1 year | ~60% |
| 3 years | ~70% |
| 5 years | ~80% |
| 10 years | ~85% |
| 15 years | ~90% |
| 20 years | ~92% |

The pattern: short-horizon results are noisy, but over a working career, the active fund universe trails the benchmark essentially completely. Specific funds beat it; the average doesn't.

The SAME pattern holds in international equities, small-caps, fixed income (mostly), and emerging markets. Active managers persistently underperform their benchmarks net of fees, across geographies and styles.

## Why active typically loses

Three reasons, in roughly the order of their importance:

1. **Fees**. The 1.0% active ER vs 0.05% passive ER is 95 bp of guaranteed annual drag for the active manager to overcome.
2. **Costs the ER doesn't show**: transaction costs from turnover, market impact on size, tax inefficiency from frequent realized gains. These add another 50-100 bp for typical actively-managed equity funds.
3. **Zero-sum gross of fees**: even gross of fees, active managers as a group earn the market average (by point 1 of Bogle's argument). So they need to be among the BETTER half of active managers gross of fees to break even against passive net of fees. Persistence studies show this is rare.

## Persistence: can past performance predict future winners?

The natural defense of active is "pick the GOOD active managers." Empirically:

- Funds in the TOP QUARTILE for the previous 5 years have only a 20-30% chance of staying in the top quartile for the next 5 years. Random would be 25%.
- Funds in the TOP DECILE have similar reversion: most fall out within 5-10 years.
- The exceptions (Buffett, Lynch's Magellan, Klarman) are visible BECAUSE they're exceptions; they don't form a screenable category in advance.

The implication for retail: even if you believed active CAN win, you can't reliably identify in advance which managers will. Selection costs and selection errors typically exceed the alpha of the actual winners.

## Three honest caveats to the passive case

The passive argument is strong but not absolute. Three legitimate refinements:

### 1. Passive cap-weighted indexes have factor exposures of their own

Cap-weighted indexes mechanically tilt toward whatever is currently expensive: bubble winners, megacaps. In the late 1990s, the S&P 500 became increasingly tech-heavy at exactly the wrong moment. In 2024, AAPL+MSFT+GOOGL+NVDA+AMZN+META exceeded 30% of the S&P 500.

This isn't a critique of passive per se, but of CAP-WEIGHTED passive specifically. Equal-weighted, factor-tilted, or fundamental-weighted variants exist and have their own empirical record (mixed; some marginal alpha but with higher costs).

### 2. Inefficient markets may genuinely reward active

US large-cap is exceptionally efficient (thousands of full-time analysts, instant pricing, vast research). Small-cap, emerging market, distressed credit, niche real estate, private equity: these have more dispersion and (arguably) more room for skill to matter. Empirically: small-cap active funds beat their benchmarks somewhat more often than large-cap active. The premium is small and uncertain.

### 3. Some retail-skill premium is real

A small fraction of skilled retail traders (perhaps 1-5%) earn persistent alpha after costs. Studies (Barber & Odean) consistently show this is a tiny minority, mostly explained by either survivorship bias or short windows. The base rate of skill at retail level is very low.

## What this means for our work

For the paper-trading-longcall project, we're explicitly running an ACTIVE STRATEGY. The diversification gate (lesson 3.8) and the strategy-validated-rules KB exist to manage the risk that we're paying for fake alpha. We should:

- Benchmark ourselves against the S&P 500 (the obvious passive comparator).
- Account for our friction (commissions, slippage, taxes).
- Be honest if and when the strategy stops beating buy-and-hold net of those costs.

The default option is always "give up the active strategy and buy SPY." That's not failure; it's the rational fallback when the active hypothesis stops paying.
