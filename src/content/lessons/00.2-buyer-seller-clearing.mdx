## Markets clear at the margin

Suppose three buyers will pay at most $12, $10, and $8 for one widget. Three sellers will accept at minimum $7, $9, and $11. Where does the market clear?

The clearing price is the one where supply equals demand. Sort buyers descending and sellers ascending. The two cross between $9 and $10 — two trades happen, at whatever the negotiated price is in that band. The third buyer (who'd pay $8) and the third seller (who'd accept $11) do not trade.

What did the price *not* care about?

- Whether the $12 buyer is wealthier than the $8 buyer.
- Whether the $7 seller spent their lifetime mastering widget-craft.
- Whether the trade is "fair."

The clearing price is set by the marginal buyer and marginal seller — the ones who are *almost* not in the market. Move either of them, and the price moves. The infra-marginal participants are not setting the price; they are getting consumer surplus or producer surplus.

## Why marginalism matters in real markets

When commentators say "stocks are at all-time highs, that's not sustainable," they are usually thinking about averages. The market doesn't care about averages. It cares about who is just-barely-still-buying. If the marginal buyer in a roaring bull market becomes someone with a pulse and a Robinhood account, the market is structurally fragile. If the marginal buyer in a bear market is a forced seller meeting another forced seller, the market is structurally elastic to good news.

The same logic explains why insider selling can move a stock 10% on tiny volume: when the marginal seller is someone who *knows*, the market reprices fast.

## Auctions and continuous markets

The clearing logic is clearest in an auction (one moment, one match), but continuous markets are just rolling auctions. NYSE has an opening auction (call market) at 9:30 ET and a closing auction at 4:00 ET. In between, it runs as a continuous double auction with a central limit order book. We'll come back to the order book mechanics in Layer 4.

## Carrying forward

- The clearing price is set at the margin, not the average.
- The unsexy-but-true reason markets move: someone went from not-in to in (or vice versa) at the margin.
- "Stocks are too high" is a claim about averages; "the marginal buyer is now retail without an income statement" is a claim about clearing fragility. The second is what matters.
