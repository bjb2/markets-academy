## Markets clear at the margin

Sort buyers descending by what they'll pay; sort sellers ascending by what they'll accept. They walk toward each other. The trade happens where they meet — and stops the moment the next-best buyer falls below the next-best seller's reservation price.

The clearing price is set by the **marginal buyer** and **marginal seller** — the ones who are almost not in the market. Move either of them, and the price moves. The infra-marginal participants (the $12 buyer, the $7 seller) get *surplus* — they would have happily transacted at less-favorable terms.

## Why marginalism matters in real markets

When commentators say "stocks are at all-time highs, that's not sustainable," they are usually thinking about averages. The market doesn't care about averages. It cares about who is just-barely-still-buying. If the marginal buyer in a roaring bull market becomes someone with a pulse and a Robinhood account, the market is structurally fragile. If the marginal buyer in a bear market is a forced seller meeting another forced seller, the market is structurally elastic to good news.

The same logic explains why insider selling can move a stock 10% on tiny volume: when the marginal seller is someone who *knows*, the market reprices fast.

## Auctions and continuous markets

The clearing logic is clearest in an auction (one moment, one match), but continuous markets are just rolling auctions. NYSE has an opening auction (call market) at 9:30 ET and a closing auction at 4:00 ET. In between, it runs as a continuous double auction with a central limit order book. We'll come back to the order book mechanics in Layer 4.
