## What the Fed is

The Federal Reserve is the US central bank. It has three official mandates from Congress: maximum employment, stable prices, and moderate long-term interest rates. In practice the first two ("dual mandate") drive almost all decisions.

Structurally, the Fed is a federation of 12 regional reserve banks plus a Board of Governors in Washington. Monetary policy is set by the **FOMC** (Federal Open Market Committee), 12 voting members: 7 governors + the NY Fed president (permanent) + 4 other regional presidents on annual rotation. The FOMC meets 8 times a year.

The Chair (currently Powell) chairs the FOMC and is the public face. The Chair doesn't dictate policy, votes are real and dissents happen, but the Chair has enormous agenda-setting and signaling power.

## What the Fed actually does

Four main tools:

1. **Set the federal funds rate target.** This is the rate at which banks lend reserves to each other overnight. The FOMC announces a target range (e.g., 5.25–5.50%). Almost all other rates (consumer loans, mortgages, corporate bonds) are pegged to this rate plus a spread.
2. **Pay interest on bank reserves (IORB).** Since 2008 this is the actual mechanism that pins the fed funds rate. The Fed pays banks a rate on reserves held at the Fed; banks won't lend to each other for less.
3. **Open market operations**, buy and sell Treasury securities (and post-2008, MBS). Buying adds reserves to the system. Selling drains reserves. QE was the term used when these purchases became large and ongoing.
4. **Lender of last resort.** In crises the Fed provides emergency lending to solvent-but-illiquid institutions (the discount window, plus various special facilities created on the fly, TALF, PDCF, etc.).

The Fed does NOT control: long-term rates directly (these are market-set), fiscal policy (Congress), or any specific market's level.

## Reading the Fed

The Fed communicates via:

- **FOMC statement** (released at 2pm ET on meeting days). Short, every word is parsed. A two-word change can move markets 1%.
- **Powell press conference** (30 min after the statement). Q&A. Sometimes more informative than the statement.
- **Dot plot** (quarterly). Each FOMC member's projection of the appropriate fed funds rate over the next few years.
- **FOMC minutes** (3 weeks after the meeting). More detailed; reveals the range of views on the committee.
- **Speeches by governors and presidents.** Public, sometimes coordinated.
