## The CAPM equation

The Capital Asset Pricing Model, derived independently by Sharpe (1964), Lintner (1965), and Mossin (1966) from Markowitz's foundation, states:

`E[R_i] = R_f + β_i · (E[R_m] − R_f)`

Where:
- R_f: risk-free rate (typically 3-month T-bill)
- E[R_m]: expected market return (typically the broad equity index)
- β_i = Cov(R_i, R_m) / Var(R_m): the stock's sensitivity to the market
- (E[R_m] − R_f): the equity risk premium

In words: a stock's required return is the risk-free rate PLUS a premium proportional to its market-beta. A stock with β = 1 must earn the market return. A stock with β = 2 must earn TWICE the market premium above R_f. A stock with β = 0 (truly uncorrelated with the market) must only earn R_f.

This is a startling claim. It says **only systematic (market) risk is priced**. Idiosyncratic risk specific to one company is diversifiable, so investors won't pay for it. If you bear it, you bear it for free.

## What beta actually measures

β_i is the slope of a regression of stock i's returns on market returns. Run the line, get the slope, call it beta. Computationally trivial. Conceptually:

- β > 1: stock amplifies market moves. Tech, leveraged businesses, small caps.
- β ≈ 1: stock moves with the market. Diversified industrials.
- 0 < β < 1: stock dampens market moves. Utilities, staples, some defensive sectors.
- β = 0: stock is uncorrelated with market. Rare. Some merger-arb strategies, T-bills.
- β < 0: stock moves opposite the market. Gold miners in some regimes; some long-vol strategies.

A common mistake: confusing β (covariance, scale-aware) with ρ (correlation, scale-free). A stock with ρ = 1 but vol = 2× market has β = 2. ρ tells you about co-movement direction; β tells you about co-movement magnitude.

## The Security Market Line

A useful picture: plot β on the x-axis, expected return on the y-axis. CAPM says every stock should plot on a single line:

- y-intercept: R_f
- slope: equity risk premium (E[R_m] − R_f)
- the market portfolio plots at (β = 1, E[R_m])

This line is the **Security Market Line (SML)**. Stocks above the SML are "cheap" (offering more return than their β requires); stocks below are "expensive." Active management is, in part, the search for SML mispricings.

The SML differs from the Capital Allocation Line (CAL) you saw in lesson 3.3: CAL is in (σ, μ) space and is about portfolios; SML is in (β, μ) space and is about individual assets.

## Why CAPM matters even if it's wrong

CAPM has been empirically attacked since the early 1970s. The two big failures:

1. **The flat slope.** Empirical SML is too flat: high-β stocks earn less than CAPM predicts, low-β stocks earn more. The "low-vol anomaly" is one of the most robust findings in factor research.
2. **β alone is not enough.** Fama and French (1992, next lesson) showed that size and value characteristics explain return variation that β doesn't capture. Multi-factor models replaced single-factor CAPM in academic and quantitative practice.

So CAPM is wrong in its predictive precision. But it remains:

- The **discount-rate framework** used in virtually all corporate-finance valuation (every DCF you'll see has CAPM behind its cost-of-equity number).
- The **conceptual benchmark** for everything that came after. Factor models extend CAPM; behavioral finance critiques CAPM; risk parity inverts CAPM's volatility intuitions. All of them assume you know CAPM.
- The **first vocabulary** for systematic vs idiosyncratic risk, β, and risk-adjusted return.

You'll spend years in markets without using CAPM to price anything precisely. You won't go a week without using the words it gave you.

## A subtle but important point

CAPM's central claim is not "high β predicts high return in your data." It is "high β REQUIRES high expected return for the market to be in equilibrium." The two are different. The equilibrium reading is conceptual; the predictive reading is empirical. The empirical reading has failed. The equilibrium reading is still useful as a baseline assumption from which deviations can be measured and traded against. This is the same move <SourceLink id="markowitz-1952" /> makes: the framework is normative (how the world should look) rather than descriptive (how it does look).
