## Why heterodox macro is worth reading

Mainstream macroeconomics (the kind you'd get in a typical undergrad course) is a useful baseline but has structural gaps. It tends to:

- Treat money as neutral over the long run, which sidesteps the question of *who gets the new money first*.
- Assume central banks are independent and effective, which is partly true but masks fiscal-monetary coordination dynamics.
- Underweight credit and balance-sheet dynamics (the Minsky / Mehrling territory).
- Pattern-match poorly to regime transitions because models are calibrated to recent regimes.

Heterodox voices fill these gaps. None are individually right about everything. The point isn't to swap orthodoxies; it's to hold multiple frames in tension and choose the one that fits a given question.

## The voices worth knowing

**Perry Mehrling — monetary plumbing.** Already covered. Mehrling is the most operationally accurate framework for how money actually moves. Not really "heterodox" so much as "the part of macro that orthodox courses skip."

**Lyn Alden — debasement trade, regime transitions, global macro.** <SourceLink id="lyn-alden-newsletter" />. Alden is bullish hard assets / bitcoin / commodities on a fiscal-dominance thesis. Whether or not you buy the thesis, her writing makes the mechanics explicit in a way most macro doesn't.

**Cullen Roche — Pragmatic Capitalism.** <SourceLink id="pragmatic-capitalism" />. Roche is good on Modern Monetary Realism (not full MMT), the operational reality of how the Fed and Treasury interact, and why most "money printing" rhetoric is sloppy. Roche tends to be a calming counterweight to inflationary doom takes.

**MMT (Mosler, Kelton, Wray).** Modern Monetary Theory. Treats sovereign currency issuers as fundamentally different from households. Useful for understanding the operational mechanics even if you reject the political prescriptions. Has been more correct about US fiscal capacity than many predicted (no debt crisis), less correct about inflation (2021–22 was a stress test it didn't ace).

**Austrian school (Hayek, Mises, modern: Roche-adjacent skeptics).** Skeptical of central banking generally; emphasizes malinvestment from artificially low rates. Useful prior on cycle dynamics; less useful as a real-time tactical guide.

**Ray Dalio — long debt cycles.** Dalio's "How the Economic Machine Works" and the longer debt-cycle frame are widely read. Calibrate Dalio's signal-to-noise: he's good on cycles, weaker on real-time market calls.

## What to actually do with this

Read 2–3 of these voices alongside the official Fed data and FOMC text. When their views agree, weight that signal. When they disagree, watch the SPECIFIC mechanism each is pointing to — usually the disagreement reveals an open empirical question, not a settled one.

The 2020s have been an unusually good period to read these voices, because regime questions have been live. Inflation was a stress test for MMT, debt-sustainability is one for Austrian-flavored critics, the dollar/bitcoin debate is one for Alden's frame. Watching how each voice handles being partly right and partly wrong is itself an education.
