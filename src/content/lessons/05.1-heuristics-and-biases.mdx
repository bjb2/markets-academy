## The 1974 paper

<SourceLink id="kahneman-tversky-1974" /> ("Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases") is the foundational paper. The central claim: when humans estimate probabilities or make judgments under uncertainty, they use MENTAL SHORTCUTS (heuristics) that produce predictable, systematic biases.

The three heuristics they identified:

1. **Representativeness**: judging probability by similarity to a stereotype, ignoring base rates.
2. **Availability**: judging frequency by ease of recall, not by actual frequency.
3. **Anchoring & adjustment**: starting from an anchor (often arbitrary) and adjusting insufficiently.

These weren't proposed as exhaustive; later research added dozens more (lesson 5.2 explores them). The methodological claim was the breakthrough: biases are systematic, not random; they follow rules; they can be experimentally manipulated.

## A representative example

Tversky and Kahneman's famous "Linda problem":

> "Linda is 31, single, outspoken, very bright. Studied philosophy. Concerned with discrimination and social justice. Participated in antinuclear demonstrations.
>
> Which is more probable?
> (a) Linda is a bank teller.
> (b) Linda is a bank teller AND active in the feminist movement."

About 85% of participants choose (b). But by the conjunction rule of probability, P(A AND B) ≤ P(A). The probability of (b) cannot exceed the probability of (a). Choosing (b) is provably wrong.

What's happening: subjects judge "which is more PROBABLE" by similarity to the description (representativeness). Linda sounds like a feminist; therefore "feminist bank teller" sounds more representative than just "bank teller." The conjunction rule of probability is overridden by the heuristic.

Critically: this isn't an error that goes away when you tell people about it. Replications across decades, with statisticians, with explicit warnings, show the effect persists. Heuristic processes are FAST AND INTUITIVE; the rule-following correction (System 2) is slower and effortful.

## Why this matters for markets

Markets are a giant judgment-under-uncertainty machine. Every price reflects somebody's bet about uncertain future cash flows. If those bets are systematically biased, prices will be systematically biased.

Specific market manifestations covered in later lessons:

- **Representativeness**: growth stocks that "feel like" successful companies trade at extreme premia; cheap stocks that "feel like" losers trade at extreme discounts. The value premium (Layer 3.5) is in part the market correcting these category errors.
- **Availability**: investors over-weight recently salient events. Plane crash leads to selling airline stocks; earthquake leads to selling insurance. Market reactions to vivid news consistently overshoot.
- **Anchoring**: stock prices anchor to recent levels. "Cheap" relative to last year, "expensive" relative to last year, even when fundamentals have changed materially.

Each bias becomes a tradable pattern if (a) you can identify it reliably and (b) other market participants haven't already corrected it. Both conditions are nontrivial.

## System 1 vs System 2 (Thinking, Fast and Slow)

Kahneman's 2011 popular book formalized the dual-process framework:

| System | Mode | Speed | Effort | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **System 1** | Intuitive, automatic | Fast | Low | Driving on a familiar route, recognizing a face, completing "two plus two..." |
| **System 2** | Deliberate, analytical | Slow | High | Mental arithmetic with multi-digit numbers, evaluating an argument, learning a new skill |

The heuristics-and-biases program demonstrated that System 1 dominates most decisions, INCLUDING those we think we're making with System 2. We rationalize System 1 conclusions post-hoc with System 2 reasoning. The bias remains.

For investing, this means: even careful, deliberate-feeling investment decisions often have System 1 anchors that the rational story papered over. Recognizing your own System 1 patterns (lesson 5.4 on calibration) is the practical entry point.

## What's NOT in the biases catalog

Three things behavioral finance is NOT:

1. **Pop-psychology applied to markets.** Each of the documented biases is from controlled experiments with replication, not from observation that "people sometimes panic."
2. **A general claim that markets are inefficient.** The standard read is that biases exist AND markets impound them (eventually); the question is whether they CLEAR before opportunities are tradable.
3. **An excuse for any narrative.** "It went up because of greed" and "it went down because of fear" are not behavioral explanations; they are tautologies. A behavioral explanation requires identifying a specific bias mechanism and (ideally) predicting its persistence.
